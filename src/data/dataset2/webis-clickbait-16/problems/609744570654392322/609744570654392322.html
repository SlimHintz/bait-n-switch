            College World Series features stars, firepower but no Cinderellas

            OMAHA, Neb. -- The familiar green and orange uniforms arrived Wednesday aboard the jet that carried the Miami (Florida) Hurricanes to the destination that once served as their de facto annual home in June.

            Has it really been seven years?

            Omaha built a new stadium for the College World Series and has crowned four champions at TD Ameritrade Park, barely accounting for half of the length of the Hurricanes' absence from an event it once owned -- or at least held a share of ownership with the likes of LSU and Cal State Fullerton.

            "Coming to the stadium the first day we got here was pretty surreal," Miami third baseman David Thompson said Friday as his teammates completed practice on the eve of the CWS. "We've seen it on TV but didn't realize how many seats there actually were here.

            "And just seeing all these people who are so nice to us, it's been awesome."

            Fifth-seeded Miami plays in its first CWS since 2008 -- and 24th overall since 1974 -- Saturday night at 8 ET on ESPN against fourth-seeded Florida. Arkansas and Virginia, the runners-up last year, open the double-elimination tournament Saturday at 3 p.m. ET, also on ESPN.

            The 69th College World Series features an intriguing mix of traditional college baseball powers and the best of rising programs. Half of the field comes from the Southeastern Conference, matching a record for representation by one league, and all four of the finalists for the Golden Spikes Award -- presented to the nation's top amateur players -- are with their teams in Omaha.

            So yes, this CWS is heavy on firepower. Never, though, according to the coaches and players in attendance, will anything overshadow the atmosphere, established over decades, that weathered the move downtown from Rosenblatt Stadium in 2011.

            "When you're a young coach," LSU's Paul Mainieri said, "which I was many years ago, you would lay in bed at night and say, 'Oh, dear God, if you would ever let me take a team to Omaha, I'll never ask for anything else.' And then you get to come here and you see how cool it is, and you say, 'Well, I kinda fibbed to you, I'd like to bring another team.'

            "I'm fortunate this is my fifth time here, and I can tell you, it never gets old. For every college player, every coach, every staff member, this is the ultimate dream."

            The Tigers, seeded No. 2 overall, fall into the category of CWS royalty. LSU has won six titles, most recently in 2009, in 16 trips to Omaha, but it has appeared just once at this stadium and has yet to win a game at TD Ameritrade Park.

            For Miami, after the longest drought in school history, and 17-time visitor Fullerton of the Big West, this year marks a first visit to the new venue. The Titans last made it to the CWS in 2009.

            "I don't have any experience being here as a head coach," said Fullerton's Rick Vanderhook, a longtime assistant at the school who took over in 2012. "My players have no experience being here."

            Vanderhook said he has talked to other coaches about "how overwhelming this can be." And that ranks as one of the key challenges at the CWS -- to play in a super-charged environment and maintain focus.

            Florida catcher and designated hitter JJ Schwarz said he took a few tips from his sister, Taylor Schwarz, the junior first baseman on the Gator's national-championship softball team.

            "She called me and told me there's going to be tons of people," said Schwarz, named this week as the top freshman nationally by the National Collegiate Baseball Writers Association. "In the end, it's just you versus the pitcher. It's just the game of baseball. It's not life or death."

            Good advice for all in Omaha.

            "I've never played in front of a crowd like this," Schwarz said, "so I think it's going to be new."

            Vanderbilt returns after defeating Virginia for the program's first national title a year ago. The Commodores, who swept a super regional at sixth-seeded Illinois, open play Sunday night at 8 ET against Fullerton.

            No. 2-seeded LSU will play No. 7 TCU Sunday at 3 p.m ET.

            Back for a second straight year and third appearance in six seasons, the Horned Frogs still look like new blood compared to LSU. Virginia, making its fourth trip under coach Brian O'Connor since 2009, and Vanderbilt, in its third visit, match the description.

            Arkansas, in its eighth trip overall and fourth since 2004, and Florida, with nine visits overall and four in the past six years, fall in the middle.

            Clearly, there is no Cinderella in town this year.

            "When you're banging on the door every year and you get here in consecutive years, that's not by accident," Mainieri said. "That's a program. That's different than just having a good team."

            As usual, keep an eye on the SEC teams. The league has won four of the past six titles and placed a team in the finals for seven straight years.

            "We're prideful of that, [but] we don't bang ourselves on the chest and say, 'This is the toughest conference,'" Vanderbilt coach Tim Corbin said. "It's a very good conference, and I think you have to endure it.

            "If you do, I think you're prepared. You're prepared for a regional. You're prepared for a super regional just because of what you have to go through."

            Vanderbilt shortstop Dansby Swanson went to the Diamondbacks with the No. 1 pick in the Major League Baseball draft this week. LSU shortstop Alex Bregman was drafted second overall by the Astros.

            Arkansas center fielder Andrew Benintendi, who won the Dick Howser Trophy on Friday, was picked seventh by the Red Sox, and Vandy right-handed pitcher Carson Fulmer went eighth to the White Sox.

            Seven participants at this CWS landed among the top 40 picks -- a star-studded field ready to write a new chapter of history.
