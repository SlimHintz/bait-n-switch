Community leaders cite little-known state law to file charges in Tamir Rice shooting

759
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Marcus Gilmer2015-06-09 14:11:21 UTC

Community leaders in Cleveland, Ohio announced on Tuesday that they've filed affidavits with the Cleveland Municipal Court seeking charges against Cleveland police officers Timothy Loehmann and Frank Garmback in the November 2014 shooting death of 12-year-old Tamir Rice.

See also: 'I'm looking for a conviction': Tamir Rice family wants to bypass grand jury

The group, including several clergy and other community leaders, made the announcement in a press conference ET Tuesday morning, invoking an obscure state law to bypass prosecutors and ask the judge directly to file charges, which include aggravated murder and manslaughter.

Rev. Jawanza Colvin, a member of the group filing the affidavits, said at today's press conference,"Today we are not taking action against police officers, we are taking action for the people. Those who take a sworn oath to protect and serve the people, protect and serve in the name of the people. And when the people believe something has been done in their name with the deeds of their public servants that is illegal and criminal, the people have the right to hold those actions and those individuals accountable."

"No lie can live forever. How long? Not long, because you shall reap what you sow." Group speaks for #TamirRice case. pic.twitter.com/9wcetiegrF

— WKYC Channel 3 News (@wkyc) June 9, 2015

The law, enacted in 1960, reads as follows:

A private citizen having knowledge of the facts who seeks to cause an arrest or prosecution under this section may file an affidavit charging the offense committed with a reviewing official for the purpose of review to determine if a complaint should be filed by the prosecuting attorney or attorney charged by law with the prosecution of offenses in the court or before the magistrate.

While the case was handed to Cuyahoga County prosecutors last week, community leaders and family members have expressed skepticism over the process, pointing to similar recent cases, including the deaths of Eric Garner and Michael Brown, where charges were not brought against officers involved each death. Walter Madison, an attorney for the Rice family, told The New York Times, “The writing is on the wall. If you look at every other instance, it ends up unfavorable to the families.”

"'The Cleveland 8' should be commended for their pursuit of justice," Madison told Mashable of the group's strategy. "Their efforts are legitimate, transparent, and trust building in nature. These are principles, in this 21st century, worthy of emulation. The people perceive an injustice; our system provides the remedy. It's called the LAW."

The group, whose members include community activists, a pastor and a professor, released their perception of that law in documents made public on Tuesday.

Here are the people in Cleveland who are asking for arrests warrants on the 2 Police Officers. #TamirRice pic.twitter.com/Lx56095REA

— Scott Taylor (@ScottTaylor19) June 9, 2015

Rev. Colvin explained on Monday in a statement, "We are still waiting for the criminal justice system to enact justice in the name of Tamir Rice. It has been more than six months since his tragic death and, yet, the people still have no answers and no one has been held accountable."

Another member, Edward Little, said it was "justice" the group is after.

Edward Little says concerned citizens are speaking out on Tamir Rice's behalf. They want justice.

— Peggy Gallek (@PeggySinkovich) June 9, 2015

A spokesman for Cuyahoga County Prosecutor Timothy J. McGinty said yesterday, "Once the investigation is complete - and in the death of Tamir Rice, it is not at this time - all evidence and expert analysis will be presented to the Grand Jury."

He added: "The Grand Jury in Cuyahoga County, by the policy of the County Prosecutor's Office, ultimately makes the charging decision in all fatal use of deadly force cases that involve law enforcement officers."

After the press conference, Steve Loomis, president of the Cleveland Police Patrolmen's Association, released a blistering statement saying, "It is very sad how miserable the lives of these self appointed activists, civil rights leaders, and clergy must be. I can't imagine being so very consumed with anger and hatred." He added, "Civilized society cannot permit the rule of law to be subverted by mob rule."

The Cleveland Police Department has faced heavy scrutiny after a Justice Department report detailing abuses and use of excessive force by the department, revelations that led to a promise of department reform.

McGinty's office also faced criticism recently after Cleveland Police officer Michael Brelo was acquitted of charges stemming from the shooting deaths of two people in 2012.
