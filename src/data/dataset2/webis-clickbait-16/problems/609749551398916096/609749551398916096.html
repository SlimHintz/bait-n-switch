Hillary Clinton kicks off 2016 presidential campaign with populist message

1.9k
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Megan Specia2015-06-13 15:00:41 UTC

Hillary Clinton officially kicked off her second presidential campaign on Saturday with her first major rally. The event, held on New York City's Roosevelt Island, drew hundreds, as the Democratic frontrunner launches full-force toward 2016.

Clinton's speech set the tone for her campaign, and marked a sharp turn away from the roundtable discussions she has held with small groups of voters in key states since joining the race in April.

Clinton immediately made reference to the potentially historic nature of her candidacy, thanking supporters for joining her “in this beautiful park, in a place with absolutely no ceilings."

"I may not be the youngest candidate in this race, but I will be the youngest woman president and the first grandmother," she added toward the end of her speech.

Much of Clinton's speech portrayed her as the candidate for the masses: women, workers, LGBT voters and the middle class, among others. Clinton also sharply criticized the Republican Party's 2016 presidential candidates, slamming their "top-down economic policy," as well as policies that affect women and LGBT voters.

"They shame and blame women rather than respect our right to make our own decisions ... and they turn their backs on gay people who love each other,” she said to applause from the crowd. "There may be some new voices in the presidential Republican choir, but they are all singing the same old song, a song called ‘Yesterday.'"

Her populist message permeated all aspects of the rally, with Clinton outlining plans for universal preschool and quality childcare, wage equality for women and a path toward citizenship for undocumented immigrants.

She also referenced her foreign policy experience during her time as U.S. secretary of state.

"I’ve stood up to adversaries like Putin ... I was in the situation room on the day we got [Osama] bin Laden," Clinton said, before turning the discussion back to domestic issues. "I know that in order to be strong in the world, we first must be strong at home."

In an move that received thunderous applause, Clinton was joined on stage by daughter Chelsea Clinton, and husband and former U.S. President Bill Clinton.

Prior to her speech, many supporters said they hoped Clinton would announce more concrete ideas on major policy plans.

"I want a more defined vision of what she wants for the campaign," said PhuongThao Le. "I am actually opened to other candidates, maybe, that have more progressive views on the issues."

Ethan Bidna, who lives in New York, said Clinton appeals to young voters like him because she is "championing issues that we care about."

However, Bidna added that he understands why Vermont Sen. Bernie Sanders has attracted early support from young voters because unlike Clinton, he issued a clear policy statement: "I want to know what she is going to do in office, and she hasn't really said yet."

Meanwhile, others attended the rally as steadfast supporters of Clinton.

New York City Public Advocate Letitia James said Clinton's social justice record speaks for itself. As a woman, James said she also wants to "be on the right side of history" by supporting someone who could potentially become the United States' first female president.

"We need more women in politics," James said. "She wants to make sure more people have access to the ballot, and that's why I am here."

The former secretary of state teased the event and her potential campaign theme with a video released Friday. It focuses on Clinton's political record, from her earliest days with the Children's Defense Fund, to her women's rights work as first lady, and her role as New York senator following 9/11. The clip also frames her as a "fighter" for the American underdog; that term is expected to follow Clinton throughout her campaign.

Indeed, she spoke about "fighting" several times throughout her speech on Saturday: "I’ve spent my life fighting for children, families and our country, and I’m not stopping now."

After the event, Clinton heads to Iowa, and will spend the remainder of the weekend there. According to Clinton's camp, the trip will focus on building out her campaign in the key primary state.

Clinton is widely viewed as the Democratic frontrunner heading into the 2016 presidential race. Her current rivals, Sanders and former Maryland Gov. Martin O'Malley, have both already held public rallies. But while they've announced some of their own policy proposals, Clinton has remained tight-lipped on specifics.
