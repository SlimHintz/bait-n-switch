Bryan Robson says England's young players lack the toughness of European rivals

Former captain loves Rooney but fears Academy system has produced 'nice, neat players, but not winners'

Bryan Robson captaining England in Chile in 1984  Photo: GETTY

By Henry Winter, Football Correspondent

4:36PM BST 13 Jun 2015

Follow

Comments

Now this is how England should start a tournament: Paul Mariner took the kick-off, Trevor Francis nudged the ball back and Ray Wilkins released Steve Coppell on the right. Maxime Bossis conceded the throw which Mick Mills was about to take before Coppell intervened. Terry Butcher flicked the ball on and there was Bryan Robson, timing his run perfectly to score with an athletic, left-footed half-volley.

The famous sight of Robson launching himself into France after 27 seconds, eventually scoring twice in that 3-1 victory at San Mames in Bilbao, should be etched in the minds of the current England generation hurtling towards the European Championships in France.


Robson scoring what was then the fastest World Cup goal, against France in 1982 (SSPL).

Cruising through qualifying is all well and good and really to be expected; imposing themselves on a tournament is another matter and England have failed far too frequently. Now 58, Robson wants those now wearing the shirt he donned with such distinction on 90 occasions to seize the moment more, to express themselves more.

“I don’t know what it is with the boys,’’ reflected the former England captain. “It looks like a mental block as if we can’t express ourselves when we get to the final stages. The last couple of World Cups have been like that. England were disappointing in Brazil. We know the quality the squad have but the lads haven’t performed to that standard in the finals.

“In the qualifiers they look like the players who play in the Premier League week in, week out to a great standard. When they get to the finals I just hope they can relax, express themselves and try to compete to be one of the best players in the world. I want them to give the Spanish, Germans and Dutch a really good game.

“The manager and coaching staff have a certain responsibility to relax them and get the best out of them mentally. But it also comes down to players preparing themselves mentally to not be afraid of the challenge.”


Egland's players at the 2014 World Cup, when they went out at the group stage (GETTY).

Robson worries about the Academy system, believing it fails to nurture strong characters capable of dealing with pressure. “We don’t produce people who are really determined and who are leaders. This ‘non-tackling’, ‘can only train for so many hours of the week’, and ‘only play in so many games’: are we taking the strong mentality and the physical side away from our game.

“Because of the way we’ve played at tournaments over the last 12-16 years we have to have a look at our Academies. The Germans are breeding strong, powerful players. The Spanish are producing leaders in a different way. The Dutch have always had steady players who always get them to quarter-finals, semi-finals. There is still some good talent in this country but we have to look at the Academies and say: ‘are we developing that talent in the right way?’’

England have nice, neat players but not winners, although Robson made special mention of the captain Wayne Rooney. “He leads by example. He’s physically tough, mentally tough, can score great goals or play a great pass to create a goal. He’s one of the best strikers that United or England have ever had.”

Barring Euro 2004, Rooney’s tournament record remains average at best but others also need to step up. “People like Phil Jones and Chris Smalling have to come to the fore at international level,’’ continued Robson. “They’ve been about it long enough. In midfield, Jordan Henderson has to take on the mantle, the same with Fabian Delph. They have all got to prove themselves at the highest level.”

Robson has many questions about the current crop. “Can Alex Oxlade-Chamberlain, Daniel Sturridge, Jack Wilshere and Raheem Sterling establish themselves? Can they be really dangerous and play well at the highest level? In the finals, can they prove themselves to be great players? That’s their challenge.

“Hopefully some of these young boys can really stamp their authority on a competition, and at least get to the quarter-finals or the semi-finals with a bit of flair. If you’re going to get knocked out of a competition be unlucky rather than let it be a formality.

“I always remember when I played in the 1982 World Cup. I felt we were really unlucky because of the format of the competition.”

Ron Greenwood’s side beat France, Czechoslovakia and Kuwait in the group stage at San Mames, then drew in the second group stage with West Germany and Spain at the Bernabeu and went out.

“You play five games, don’t get beaten, only concede one goal (to Gerard Soler of France) and yet get knocked out at the quarter-final stage. If we’d had Kevin Keegan fit we could really have challenged to win the competition.”

Yet Robson shone. “Where you make your reputation is when you get to competitions. You have to play well. I got good reports when I came back; obviously scoring the quickest goal in World Cup history gave me a lot of publicity (it was broken by Turkey’s Hakan Sukur in 2002). It gave me so much more confidence when I went back into the United dressing-room. That was one of my best years coming off the back of a World Cup.” Only Frank Stapleton with 19 goals scored more for United than Robson’s 15 in 92-93.

“If you can prove yourself at the European Championships or the World Cup it does give individual players real confidence that you’ve competed against some of the best players in the world, you’ve challenged them, and done well against them.”

Life changed for Robson in 82. “I came back from the World Cup, United had just made me captain, England made me captain, and my world changed with the extra publicity, the responsibilities. My profile went up so much. Everybody wanted a piece of me.”

Paul Gascoigne experienced even more when returning from Italia 90. “As Paul says, everybody scrutinises everything you do. Gazza was a really great player, the best I ever played with from 1990 to 96, , a character and a half. He just wanted everyone to love him.”


Paul Gascoigne, rated by Robson the best player he played with, was in huge demand after Italia 90, meeting prime minister Margaret Thatcher (STEPHEN LOCK).

Robson was more even-keeled, taking fame in his stride in 82. “I had a balance. I’d just had a newborn baby and we’d already had the first daughter. I became more mature with a family to support. I realised that you can’t go nightclubbing. It gives you that extra responsibility.”


Robson says returning to wife Denise, and daughters Claire and Charlotte kept him grounded after the 1982 World Cup (REX).

Robson has never shirked responsibility. He is still delivering for United. At Old Trafford on Sunday, Robson manages a United Legends team against Bayern Munich AllStars coached by Paul Breitner, one of his adversaries at the 82 World Cup.

“Last year when we played in Munich we raised Â£950,000 which goes to help children from underprivileged areas,’’ said Robson of this annual charity match. “The Manchester United Foundation don’t just bring people off the street, they educate them to go on and have jobs rather than just walk the streets.”

The game is preceded by a concert from Union J and X-Factor winner Ben Haenow and Ella Eyre. “With the bands it will be a day out for the kids,’’ said Robson. “It should be a really exciting game for the fans. We’ve got Jaap Stam, Louis Saha, (Ji-sung) Park, Scholesy, Phil Neville. Then I’ve also got the (older) lads, fit boys who’ve looked after themselves like Denis Irwin, Andy Cole, Dwight Yorke, Quinton Fortune and Edwin van der Sar.”

With walk-up on the day, United hope for 50,000-plus, building on the feelgood factor generated by Louis van Gaal. “He’s done a very good job to steady the ship in his first year,’’ observed Robson. “If anybody had said at the start of the season that we’d get back in the Champions League, even though we do have to win the qualifier, most United fans would have accepted that.

“Another top-drawer central midfield player who can dictate the pace of the game would be a great signing. We don’t have another player like Michael Carrick. Scholesy could do it before to a certain degree but even Scholesy was more attacking than defensive like Michael. Scholes was a great player, great vision, a great passer of a ball with that timing to get into the box to score a goal. He’s one of the best midfield players England have ever produced.

“When Michael is injured we don’t really have that player who can dictate the pace of the game like Scholesy could; before that it was Keaney, Nicky Butt, myself and Incey. That’s why everyone at United keeps going on about having aggressive, box-to-box players because they got used to it from the early 80s to 2000.” There will be one in the Old Trafford dug-out on Sunday. How England could do with another Bryan Robson on the pitch.
