Cheese porn alert! The 10 best things to do with cheese

From parmesan ice cream to cheese and champagne, here are the 10 things every cheese-lover should have on their bucket list

Say cheese! A gorgeous selection from cheesemonger Buchanans 

By Patrick McGuigan

7:00AM BST 11 Jun 2015

Comments

Goat's cheese and green tea

Step away from the Hobnobs – cheese is what you should be nibbling with a cuppa (although dunking is not advised). It might sound a strange match, but the floral, fruity notes found in some teas are similar to those in many cheeses. Cheesemonger Buchanans likes to pair Golden Cross goats' cheese with green tea from Lalani & Co. It's an inspired choice - the floral notes in the cheese complementing the herbaceous tea.

Poutine

It looks like a terrible accident in a chip factory, but poutine is actually a Canadian delicacy combining fries, gravy and cheese curds. It's become a bit of 'thing' in the UK with restaurants, such as Hawksmoor Spitalfields, putting their own spin on the dish. Chef Richard Turner makes his with triple cooked chips covered in cows' milk curds and either pig head meat or chicken gravy and crispy chicken skin. If you're tempted to try making it at home, you can buy curd from Neal's Yard Dairy.

Canadian favourite: poutine

Parmesan ice cream

The idea of cheese-flavoured ice cream is nothing new with recipes incorporating Parmesan dating back to the 18th century. Criterion Ices in Suffolk has revived the tradition with combinations including white chocolate and Stilton, and Gorgonzola and port. It's seasonal Parmigiano ice cream is a favourite with chefs, but the company says it can easily be made at home by adding grated Parmesan to hot cream and sugar. Serve with chilled tomato soup.

A scoop of parmesan ice cream

British raclette

Cheese addicts don't have to visit the Alps to get their raclette fix. There's a British version of the pungent Swiss cheese, which is grilled until it reaches a lava-like state before being blanketed over potatoes. Called Ogleshield, it's made with rich Jersey cows' milk in Somerset and was invented (and named after) former Neal's Yard maturer Bill Oglethorpe. He now serves up plates of molten Ogleshield raclette at his Kappacasein stall at Borough market. Creamy and fruity with a wine-like flavour, the melted cheese is like an edible duvet – pure comfort.

Bill Oglethorpe serving up a delicious dish of raclette

Grilled peach and goats' cheese

A simple summery dish that works as a starter or a pudding. Cut a ripe peach in half, remove the stone and place skin-side up under a hot grill for 2-3 minutes, then flip over and grill the other side for same amount of time. Fill the hole left by the stone with soft, creamy goat's cheese (try Rosary), and put back under the grill for a minute to warm through. Serve with basil leaves and a dash of balsamic. You can buy Rosary at Waitrose

Sloe gin and blue cheese

The great gin revival has parallels with the renaissance in British cheese so it seems natural to put the two together. Paxton & Whitfield recommend Sipsmith sloe gin with a slice of Cornish Blue, which was named supreme Champion at the World Cheese Awards in 2010. The sweet, salty cheese is wrapped up in the velvety texture and blackcurrant flavours of the gin.

Match made in heaven?

Baked figs with Comté and dark chocolate

Yes, you read it right. Cheese and chocolate can work well together, and both have an affinity with figs. A 10-minute dinner party canapé or just a posh Wednesday afternoon snack, these are ridiculously easy to make. Stuff dried figs (or fresh when in season) with a little piece of young Comté and dark chocolate (fruity Green and Blacks 70 per cent is good) then bake in a hot oven until melted. Can be pimped with toasted almonds, honey and mascarpone.

Marinated feta

Feta can be given a new lease of life by marinating it in olive oil with whatever herbs and spices you fancy. Chop the cheese into cubes and place in sterilised jars with fresh thyme, chilli flakes, peppercorns and lemon zest (garlic is best avoided). Fill with olive oil and store in the fridge for up to a week. If that's too much hassle, you can always buy a jar of Graceburn, made in south London by Blackwoods. Buy from Natoora.

Thing of beauty: Blackwoods' marinated cheese

Champagne and Brillat-Savarin

Champagne or indeed English sparkling wine is a surprisingly good match for rich, buttery cheeses. The acidity and bubbles cut through the creaminess, rebooting the palate for the next mouthful. There are few cheeses richer or creamier than Brillat-Savarin - a French triple cream brie named after the famous 19th century gourmand. Try it for yourself at champagneplusfromage.co.uk

Burrata and just about anything

A trendy cousin of mozzarella with an outrageously gooey centre, burrata's pure milky notes are like a blank canvas for other flavours. Chefs love its versatility, pairing it with an array of ingredients in everything from starters and desserts to pizzas and salads. Florence Knight peps hers up with chilli and agretti (succulent, salty Italian leaves), while Yotam Ottolenghi matches burrata with blood orange and peach, plus coriander seeds, at Nopi. Basically, anything goes.

Outrageously gooey: burrata, as served at Nopi
