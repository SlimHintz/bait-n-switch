11 reasons why women's football is better than men's

England’s Women’s World Cup campaign continues on Saturday night, as they face Mexico in their second group game (9pm KO on BBC Three). Here’s our teamful of reasons why it’s superior to the men’s game …

England have a chance to make amends against Mexico after narrowly losing out to France earlier this week 

By Michael Hogan

11:31AM BST 13 Jun 2015

Comments

1. The players are less hate-able

One of the main problems with top-level men’s football is the insufferable players. There are exceptions, sure, but on the whole they’re overpaid, over-entitled, oversexed, over-styled spoilt brats. All ostentatious bling, designer logos, phallic sports cars, mock-Tudor mansions, “banter”, “roasting”, sleeve tattoos and hair gel. The women’s game isn’t blighted with nearly as much preening, posing or all-round prickery. Although the odd one still does “heart-hands” when they score.

England captain Steph Houghton and Liverpool wantaway winger Raheem Sterling

  • Women’s World Cup 2015: The cheat sheet you need

2. There’s less play-acting and time-wasting

The men’s game is embarrassingly cynical, with the big wusses diving and feigning injury if someone so much as farts in their general direction. Women’s football is far more phlegmatic. A study shows that on average, men stay down 30 seconds longer than women after being fouled. It’s the sporting equivalent of us crying “man flu” the moment we get a slight sniffle.

The women’s game also features more actual action. The same study found that men take 10 seconds longer to get off the field when substituted and 30 seconds longer celebrating a goal.

Rivaldo recovers after being hit in the head leg at the 2002 World Cup



3. The money is less obscene

It’s supply and demand, obv, but the wages in men’s football are queasily astronomical. Cristiano Ronaldo earns a £300k-per-week base salary, and that’s before endorsements, bonuses and prize money. Compare this to England’s women, who are paid £23k per year by the FA – pretty much bang on the national average wage. Adding on their club contracts and commercial endorsements still only totals £50k. Yep, they earn in a year what the likes of Wayne Rooney and John Terry do in a day.

Unlike Premier League money-grabbers – forever holding their clubs to ransom and happy to warm the bench if it pays well – women still play for love of the game, not their bank balances.

Cristiano Ronaldo illustrates the amoung of millions he's made just by getting out of bed that day



4. It’s innocent and untainted

The next two men’s World Cups will stink of corruption: backhanders, bribes, dodgy deals struck on yachts over prawn sandwiches. This Women’s World Cup is the first post-Blatter tournament and, after the FIFA scandal left a sour taste, something of a palate-cleanser.

It’s fitting that Sepp Blatter stepped down before kick-off because he’s unapologetically sexist. In 2004, the daft old chauvinist said female players should wear “tighter shorts” to appeal to male viewers.



5. There’s more variety and competition

The men’s World Cup has only ever been won by nations from South America or Europe. The same four usual suspects – Brazil, Argentina, Italy, Germany – tend to win everything. Only France, Spain and England have come from outside that quartet to win since 1950.

By contrast, despite the tournament only being 24 years old, the women’s version has already gone to three different continents. Reigning champions Japan, two-time victors USA, 1995 winners Norway and much-fancied Canada are all in with a decent shout this time.

There's a surprise: Germany celebrate winning the 2015 men's World Cup (Photo: Fifa)



6. It’s more enlightened

Men’s football is stuck in the dark ages when it comes to tolerance: blighted by homophobic chants, racism on both turf and terraces, plus a shameful lack of black managers. The women’s game has accepted its gay players without blinking, with England's Casey Stoney one of several high-profile stars to have come out. Can you imagine our men’s skipper being openly gay? Dream on.

And although previous England manager Hope Powell lost her job to a man, Mark Sampson, two years ago, eight teams competing at the current World Cup are led by women.

England's Casey Stoney (Photo: Christopher Pledger)



7. England are better

After last year’s flop trip to Brazil saw them tumble to 20th, the men’s Three Lions team are currently ranked 15th in the world. The women are ranked 6th and have never been lower than 14th. They won all 10 of their World Cup qualifiers and last September, putting 10 past Montenegro away from home (you might recall that Montenegro’s men drew 2-2 with our boys in 2012, a match in which Rooney got sent off). England women also scored 13 against Hungary in an away World Cup qualifier a decade ago. England’s men haven’t scored ten goals in a game for more than half a century.



8. England are also more successful

England’s men have never reached the final of the Euros but the women have done so twice. They also lifted the Cyprus Cup in 2009 and 2013, and twice won the Mundialito (the precursor to the Women’s World Cup) during the 80s. Since 1966, England’s men have won one sole piece of silverware: Le Tournoi (remember that?) in 1997.

England men's only trophy since 1966 didn't do much for national pride, not least because it looked like a petrol station windfloater sellotaped to chunk of MDF.



9. It’s more accessible and better value

People’s game? Pah. A Premier League season ticket costs an average of £508.55. In the Women’s Super League, it’s £32.38. Little wonder that sales last season were up 30pc. Indeed, women’s football attendances have doubled in the past decade. Last autumn, the women’s side drew a bigger crowd than the men to their equivalent Wembley friendlies: 55,000 to 40,000. Tickets for the women’s match were just £15 for adults and a quid for kids, who could support a better-ranked team in a superior atmosphere.

Fan power: Supporters stage their protest at ticket prices outside the Emirates (Photo: TWITTER)



10. It’s more feelgood

The nation back the women’s team with none of that club factionalism or world-weary “We’re rubbish/sack the manager/bet we get knocked out on penalties” eye-rolling. The press are less negative and sniping. There’s a general sense that it's all just a good thing and we should get behind our girls.

Jill Scott (centre) celebrates after scoring for England in the 2011 World Cup (Photo: Getty)



11. When they swap shirts at the final whistle

*Beavis and Butthead-style hur-hur-hur laugh* This never actually happens but it seems compulsory for blokes to make this “wise”crack when talking about women’s football. Sorry. Come on, England!
