Bloomberg to Launch New London-Based Trading Platform

  • Article
  • Comments

  • Bloomberg

  • derivatives

  • Exchanges

  • London

  • U.K.

  • By
  • Tim Cave
Zuma Press

Bloomberg, operator of some of the world’s largest platforms for fixed income trading, is in the final stages of gaining approval for a new U.K. trading system that would mark its first fully regulated European trading venue.

The firm is seeking approval for a so-called multilateral trading facility (MTF) with the U.K.’s Financial Conduct Authority, according to two people familiar with the matter. An MTF is an exchange-like venue that bring together buyers and sellers.

Bloomberg has already applied for a “market identifier code”, or MIC, for the venue, which will be called BMTF, according to filings with Swift, a bank communications network. Swift issues MICs to identify stock markets and other trading venues.

The full scope of the platform is not known, but it will initially be eligible to trade interest-rate swaps and credit derivatives, according to a person familiar with the situation.

The new platform falls under the responsibility of Nicholas Bean, head of fixed income product at Bloomberg, that person said.

The majority of MTFs were set up after new European rules that allowed competitors to challenge traditional equities exchanges. However, operators such as MarketAxess and Tradeweb, along with interdealer brokers including Icap, also established them for bonds and derivatives trading.

Bloomberg, along with Tradeweb, accounts for the lion’s share of electronic trading in European corporate bonds through its ALLQ platform, which does not have a regulatory status as a trading venue. It is regulated by the FCA as a service company, however.

According to industry practitioners, ALLQ does not have a trading venue status because it doesn’t match bids and offers, it just publishes them and allows buyers and sellers to communicate with each other.

The new platform is expected to be launched ahead of new European rules known as MIFID II. These rules will force certain types of over-the-counter derivatives, including interest-rate swaps, to be traded electronically on venues such as MTFs to help reduce risk.

U.S. regulators have already made it mandatory for swaps trading to be carried out on venues similar to MTFs, known as swap execution facilities, or SEFs. Bloomberg operates the largest SEF for credit derivatives by trading volume.
