Meet the men who are leading Britain's craft revolution

This weekend in London, a celebration of male makers will dispel the idea that crafty pursuits are only for females. Gareth May meets four of the sellers at Manmade

James Ward of Jimbob Art at work 

By Gareth May

8:06AM BST 11 Jun 2015

Comments

Real men don’t mess about with knitting and quilt making; we’re too busy pumping iron and drinking beer … Right?

A brash stereotype, yes – but despite the changing face of masculinity in the 21st Century, it's surprising how close to the truth the above statement gets. There's an explosion of craft hobbies taking hold in Britain, yet men appear to be immune to the bug.

Just look at Etsy, the Amazon for craft products, where it's estimated that only 4pc of sellers are male. Or the design fairs of London-based Crafty Fox Markets, where only around 10pc of traders are male.

What men need is some encouragement! And that’s exactly what designer-maker Mr Wingate wants to give us. He set up Manmade – a two-day event running this weekend in London in conjunction with both Etsy and Crafty Fox Markets – as a place to showcase male makers and challenge the notion that men aren’t as crafty as women.

Here we help shine a light on the men in the craft world by celebrating four of the craftiest chaps around.


Mr Wingate

Mr Wingate at work in his studio (Photo: Mr Wingate)

Full name: Sam Wingate
Age: 32
Location: East London
Craft: Printmaking
Shop: www.wingateprint.com
About: Mr Wingate first encountered screen printing at a craft fair age 12. Twenty years on he runs his own printmaking business, producing cushions and tea towels. He has worked on commissions for clients including The British Museum, Selfridges and Oxford University and he also teaches textiles at The CASS. He’s the brains behind Manmade.

Mr Wingate's lattice patterns adorn his cushions and notebooks

What do you love about craft?

The satisfaction of having an idea, working out how to produce it, and then seeing the finished article. Making samples is always the most exciting part, seeing the first incarnation. Making things in volume – that’s a different challenge and is satisfying in its own right, but can get a bit tedious.

Can craft be a masculine pursuit?

I guess contemporary craft magazines lean towards a female audience, with craft being associated with making pretty, soft items. But there is a lot of craft, which is more ‘manly’ like fishermen using crochet to mend their nets, or furniture making and woodwork. I’m not saying women can only do one sort of craft and men another, but there are crafts with a more masculine association.

Has there been a rise in crafty men?

I’m not sure that there has, yet, which is part of the reason I came up with the idea of Manmade. We are still very much in the minority when it comes to markets and craft events, but we are here! The aim of Manmade is to encourage more men to get out and start selling the things they make.


Jimbob Art

James Ward at work (Photo: Jimbob Art)

Full name: James Ward
Age: 35
Location: London
Craft: Illustrator
Shop: jimbobart.etsy.com
About: Ward was struggling to make a living as an illustrator having just finished his Illustration degree when, inspired by the homeware designs he’d seen on a trip to Stockholm – and to look busy and productive when his girlfriend’s parents were visiting – he picked up the ceramic pens and inexpensive plates he’d just bought and began drawing his characters on them. After months of experimenting he signed up to London Markets and Etsy and founded Jimbob Art.

Jimbob Art's hand-applied work

How long has it taken you to master your craft?

My craft is fairly simple as I draw on made items. I’ve always drawn animals so this just feels like a continuation of what I’ve been doing from a young age. I do enjoy finding ceramic pieces and designing a character that works with the design and form of the ceramic – whether plates, cups or one-off hand-drawn pieces.

What do you love about craft?

The range of work being created by independent crafters at the moment is staggering. It allows people to show how original, creative, and witty craft can be.

What do you say to those people who consider craft to be a ‘girlie’ pursuit?

I’m not a very ‘manly’ man and I’ve never really been interested in portraying what I do as masculine. I think I would struggle to make drawing rabbits on mugs sound macho! On the flip side, I have a lot of brilliant and talented female friends in the industry I know would question ‘girlie’ as a negative connotation! The truth is, gender really doesn’t come into it if you’re doing good work.



Zack McLaughlin

Zack McLaughin at work (Photo: zackmclaughlin.etsy.com)

Full name: Zack McLaughlin
Age: 31
Location: Shoreditch, London
Craft: Sculptor/Model maker
Website: zackmclaughlin.etsy.com
About: McLaughlin graduated in Illustration and worked for a few years illustrating Children's books. As a side project he began to write and illustrate his own book. In the book a little boy builds his very own bird lantern out of old books and willow sticks to escape the evil clutches of a monster. McLaughlin couldn't visualise how this bird lantern would look so he went about making it himself. Two years later his handmade birds sell for hundreds of pounds on Etsy.

A handmade wire robin (Photo: zackmclaughlin.etsy.com)


What do you love about your craft?

I love the whole process of designing and creating each and every bird. I love the fact I have total control of the entire process, I can look at the finished bird and confidently say that I made it.

Has being crafty benefited your life?

I really have been crafty for as long as I can remember, I don't know much else! I definitely would be lost if I couldn't create something everyday; it is a form of therapy and the fact I can do it each and every day makes me a very happy man.

Can craft be a masculine pursuit too?

The term 'crafty' does sound quite ‘girlie’ but if you think about it men have been crafting throughout history, be it sculpting, shoe making, painting, tailoring, carpentry and much more and these things seem very manly and skilled to me. I think if someone thinks men making things is ‘girlie’ then they just haven't tried it themselves and should just give it a go. I feel it is very masculine to make things, it shows ingenuity and a want to create.




Daniel Darby Jewellery

Daniel Darby at work (Photo: danieldarbyjewellery.etsy.com)

Full name: Daniel Darby
Age: 31
Location: Tooting, London
Craft: Jewellery Designer Maker
Shop: danieldarbyjewellery.etsy.com
About: Darby’s college art teacher suggested he might like to try jewellery making. He’d never considered it before but the thought of having a little shed at the bottom of the garden where he could go to make things appealed to him. More than a decade later having studied jewellery design at Buckinghamshire Chilterns University he’s one of London's most innovative jewellery makers.

A deep sea diver necklace and bicycle brooch

What do you love about craft?

Making a piece of jewellery for the first time is my favourite part of the process. Seeing the finished outcome sitting on my workbench gives me a great sense of achievement. In a wider sense I find making things with my hands very therapeutic.

Do you think there has been a rise in crafty men?

Definitely the more computerised we become the stronger the desire for handmade things. Also the shift in men’s fashion towards rugged long lasting clothing over shiny labels has had an influence. We all want good quality products with a known provenance.

What do you say to those people who consider craft to be a ‘girlie' pursuit?

Craft is for men and woman. Loads of men make things and are creative. The jewellery industry is full of men with years of experience who would happily describe themselves as craftsmen. However it is the label ‘craft’ which has become tainted; it’s seen as hobbyist, feminine and low paid which is why a lot of men are embarrassed to associate what they do with ‘craft’. Hopefully, Manmade is going to be a really interesting and fun way to challenge those ideas.



Manmade takes places on June 12 and 13 at the Truman Brewery in East London. For more information visit the Etsy blog or the Crafty Fox Market website.
