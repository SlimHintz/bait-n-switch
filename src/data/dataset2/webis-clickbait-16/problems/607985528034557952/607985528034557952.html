Slideshow: Top 10 players in the Women's World Cup

No. 1: Marta, Brazil, forward

Marta is perhaps the one player in the tournament closest to being a one-woman team in terms of how she can direct a game. With everything she has achieved and those five consecutive World Player of the Year awards up to 2010, it's hard to believe Marta's still only 29. She might be just past her peak in terms of strength and speed, but Marta's soccer intelligence is so profound that she still has a lot to teach the young pretenders -- and of course she still has unfinished business with the World Cup. -- <b><i>Graham Parker</b></i><br><br><b>The following players also received votes:</b> Camille Abily, France; Nadine Angerer, Germany; Shirley Cruz, Costa Rica; Nilla Fischer, Sweden; Laura Georges, France; Lena Goessling, Germany; Lauren Holiday, United States; Simone Laudehr, Germany; Melanie Leupolz, Germany; Carli Lloyd, United States; Vivianne Miedema, Netherlands; Anja Mittag, Germany; Aya Miyama, Japan; Yuki Ogimi, Japan; Asisat Oshoala, Nigeria; Wendie Renard, France; Celia Sasic, Germany; Becky Sauerbrunn, United States; Homare Sawa, Japan

Pitch Perfect

More than 500 players will compete in Canada with their national teams for the Women's World Cup. So which stars stand out above the rest? The experts at espnW and ESPN FC ranked their top players to help determine the best 10 players in the Women's World Cup. Forwards lead the way, making up half of the list, while seven countries are represented. Did we miss someone? Is our ranking in the right order? Head to social media and let us know what you think by tagging @espnW and using #WWC10.

(Andrea Canales, Jeff Carlisle, Julie Foudy, Ben Gladwell, Graham Hays, Kristine Lilly, Kate Markgraf, Doug McIntyre and Graham Parker participated in the voting panel.)

2 of 11

No. 10: Hope Solo, United States, goalkeeper

The consensus world's best goalkeeper can still win a game by herself. Solo has experience -- she was the Golden Glove recipient four years ago in Germany -- and while she's 33 now (and will turn 34 next month) her athleticism remains off the charts. The psychological edge she brings shouldn't be underestimated, either; her presence alone inspires confidence in teammates and fear in foes. -- Doug McIntyre

3 of 11

No. 9: Abby Wambach, United States, forward

We'll know at the end of the tournament whether the decision to effectively mothball the all-time leading goal scorer in U.S. international soccer -- to ease her through the season leading up to the World Cup -- turned out to be a stroke of genius that unleashed the power of one of the game's great competitors, or an indulgence too far for an icon of a passing era. Four years ago, a Wambach goal defined the team, though it fell short of the trophy. She's not ready to shuffle into bit part status yet, and how the U.S. technical team manage her might yet define the U.S. women's campaign again. -- Graham Parker

4 of 11

No. 8: Eugenie Le Sommer, France, midfielder

Le Sommer is the catalyst behind all that France does offensively as its playmaker and finisher. She does both very well and also has the pace to take on players and get in the penalty box on the dribble. Le Sommer is difficult to defend, as she often sits in the seam between the opponent's defenders and midfield. If Le Sommer can work her magic, the French team has enough quality around her to make a legitimate run at a World Cup title. -- Julie Foudy

5 of 11

No. 7: Dzsenifer Marozsan, Germany, midfielder

The Hungary-born midfielder is 1.FFC Frankfurt's No. 10 and played a major role in her club's Champions League triumph this season. The supremely talented playmaker, who names Cristiano Ronaldo as the footballer who most inspires her, was the youngest player to play and score in the Bundesliga, at 14 and 15 years old, respectively. Always decisive on the pitch, she scored the winning goal that led Germany to the final of EURO 2013, which it eventually won. -- Ben Gladwell

6 of 11

No. 6: Louisa Necib, France, midfielder

On a France team oozing class, Necib is the player charged with providing the creativity -- be it by running with the ball at her feet, or picking out a teammate with a deft pass. She's already got two UEFA Women's Champions League medals in her trophy case; now she'll be shooting for the ultimate international prize. -- Jeff Carlisle

7 of 11

No. 5: Lotta Schelin, Sweden, forward

Nicknamed the "female Zlatan Ibrahimovic," not just for her nationality but for her offensive qualities, "L8" has scored more goals than she has played matches with Olympique Lyonnais. She's the French club's all-time leading scorer, having won the UEFA Champions League with them in 2011 and 2012. The Sweden captain, who has made 148 appearances for her country, scoring 78 goals, is in fabulous shape and will lead a motivated team through the "group of death." -- Ben Gladwell

8 of 11

No. 4: Christine Sinclair, Canada, forward

With more than 15 years and more than 150 goals for Canada, Sinclair is synonymous with soccer in that country. She is generally quiet off the field, but outspoken on behalf of her team. Athletic and tough, the forward has helped Canada fight for a spot on the world stage against all comers, including their southern neighbor, the United States. -- Andrea Canales

9 of 11

No. 3: Veronica Boquete, Spain, midfielder

Put her in North American leagues; put her in European leagues. Squeeze her in amongst stars; shine the spotlight squarely on her. None of it matters. The Spanish No. 10 is an artist with the ball. And while Spain is new to the Women's World Cup, no midfielder in the draw offers a more proven, or more passport-tested, combination of play making and finishing. -- Graham Hays

10 of 11

No. 2: Alex Morgan, United States, forward

Morgan's rare combination of athleticism, anticipation and technical ability makes her the world's most dangerous forward when healthy. Her left foot is ruthless, but she can score with her weaker right and with her head, too. Her off-the-ball movement is brilliant -- she's a fox in the box -- and at 25, her work ethic and hunger for goals are already the stuff of legend. -- Doug McIntyre

11 of 11

No. 1: Marta, Brazil, forward

Marta is perhaps the one player in the tournament closest to being a one-woman team in terms of how she can direct a game. With everything she has achieved and those five consecutive World Player of the Year awards up to 2010, it's hard to believe Marta's still only 29. She might be just past her peak in terms of strength and speed, but Marta's soccer intelligence is so profound that she still has a lot to teach the young pretenders -- and of course she still has unfinished business with the World Cup. -- Graham Parker
