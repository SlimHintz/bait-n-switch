    • Picturing Waterloo

    Photographer Sam Faulkner aims to capture what the 1815 Battle of Waterloo really looked like

    Exhibit: ‘Unseen Waterloo—The Conflict Revisited’ Showcases More Than 80 Life-Size portraits

    By Alexandra Wolfe
    By Alexandra Wolfe

    What did the Battle of Waterloo, fought 200 years ago on June 18, 1815, really look like? There aren’t any snapshots to go by, since it was one of the last major battles fought before the invention of photography. So photographer Sam Faulkner decided to try to answer the question with his new project “Unseen Waterloo—The Conflict Revisited.”

    He spent five years visiting annual Waterloo re-enactments on the actual battlefield in Belgium and taking pictures of the participants. This summer, he’s showcasing more than 80 life-size portraits of those costumed soldiers in a new book and in an exhibition at Somerset House, an arts center in London, from June 12 through Aug. 31.

    Left: Dragoon, 5th Regiment of the Light Dragoons, the Netherlands. Right: Sapper, Imperial Guard, France.

    ‘I think the way we remember people from conflict has changed because of photography.’

    Left: Sergeant, 9th Regiment of Cuirassiers (Heavy Cavalry), France. Right: Private, 92nd (Gordon Highlanders) Regiment of Foot, Britain.

    “I think the way we remember people from conflict has changed because of photography,” says Mr. Faulkner, who lives in London. “I was quite interested in photographing what wasn’t there and capturing the idea of what it was like to step off the battlefield.” He was also fascinated by the fact that the combatants were from so many different countries. The battle marked Napoleon’s final defeat, ending over 20 years of warfare between European powers.

    Chasseur, Horse Chasseurs of the Imperial Guard, France.

    This series is a departure from Mr. Faulkner’s previous work as a war photographer. He spent years traveling to war zones, including in Afghanistan and Colombia.

    Left: Drummer, 52nd (Oxfordshire) Regiment of Foot, Britain. Right: Drummer, 44th East Essex Regiment of Foot, Britain.

    ‘I didn’t want to go shoot a re-enactment in a faux-journalistic sort of way, because that would’ve been completely contrived.’

    Left: Captain, 7th Regiment of Hussars, France. Right: Aide-de-Camp, General Army Headquarters, France.

    For Waterloo, Mr. Faulkner set up a studio at the re-enactment site so he could capture what the soldiers looked like when they returned from their mock fighting. “I didn’t want to go shoot a re-enactment in a faux-journalistic sort of way, because that would’ve been completely contrived,” he says. To make sure the uniforms were historically accurate, the re-enactors researched military fashion, from the red tunics of the Scottish Highland regiments to the piped golden dolman jackets of the Hussars.

    Aide to Division General, 2nd Regiment of Dragoons, France.

    Adding to the reality of the re-creation, the participants slept in bivouacs without sleeping bags. They used rough burlap sheets similar to those used in the early 1800s. Mr. Faulkner was grateful for a few contemporary differences. “There might have been a bit more canvas [for tents] floating around the campsite than there would’ve been in 1815,” he says, “and the food was slightly better rationed.”
