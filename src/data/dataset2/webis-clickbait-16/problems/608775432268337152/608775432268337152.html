What it's like to ride a WWII motorcycle on a frozen Siberian lake

1.3k
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Jessica Plautz2015-06-10 22:43:48 UTC

The bar for adventurous travel has been raised.

Matt Prior and Dennis Malone spent two weeks circumnavigating a frozen lake in Siberia. And as if the destination and the itinerary weren't adventurous enough, they did it in a World War II-era Russian Ural motorbike and sidecar.

See also: 10 Crazy Vacation Ideas for Thrillseekers

"I had a burning desire to do something that was on the next level compared to what I had done previously," Prior told Mashable. "I’d had a look around the world for inspiration and found it when I came across Lake Baikal, Siberia. I was in awe."

“I’ve been waiting to do something big like this for a while," said Malone.

The two were pioneering the route for The Adventurists, a team of people who are "trying to make the world less boring," according to the group.

Beginning in Irkutsk, the route went through areas even locals don't regularly visit, according to the team.

Lake Baikal is the largest freshwater lake in the world, stretching more than 430 miles (or about 700 kilometers). Temperatures dropped as low as -22 degrees Fahrenheit (-30 degrees Celsius), and winds rose to as much as 80 miles per hour. The combination was anything but traveler friendly. Even freezing temperatures are warm in Siberia, though, and that left Prior, Malone and their travel companions to battle against treacherous conditions.

But they aren't strangers to perilous adventure. Prior is a former Royal Air Force pilot, and has since made a name for himself as an adventurer.

The choice of the Ural motorcycle added another challenge to the trip, but the World War II-era, Russian-made models were symbolic.

"The locals we passed on the way to the lake, a) thought we were mad driving this heap of shit when they knew all too well how bad it was, and b) loved it as they hadn’t seen one for ages and so to see an idiot from a foreign land riding one in winter, made them smile and wave," Prior said.

He added the challenge of such an outdated piece of machinery was also part of the fun.

"It was possibly the most ridiculous and unsuitable vehicle to take," he said. "This is what made it a real challenge."

And a challenge it was.

"We’d researched the Ural online and looked over the basics and common problems -– pretty much everything, which was no surprise," said Prior. "In the end we had to practically rebuild the bike from scratch, it was that bad."

"When we first picked it up, it caught fire — twice, had no lights or brakes and wouldn’t even start."

To prepare for the trip, Prior and Malone researched the conditions of the lake. When it came to packing for Siberia, they tried to travel as light as possible.

"We looked at everything we would need, cut it down to bare essentials and made sure we could deal with worse case scenarios and then went about preparing a kit list," said Prior.

As for financing the adventure, they reached out to different brands after they had got their plans laid out, and received sponsorship from GoPro, Klim and Powertraveller.

The team was also raising money for charities, including Help for Heroes, Soldier On, Plan UK and Cool Earth, while showing that the trip was actually possible.

"Navigating the ice was a challenge in itself as it’s constantly moving and changing like tectonic plates and having never been anywhere quite like this, it was a bit of a mystery and a gamble at the same time," said Prior. "It was honestly like being dropped on a different planet."

Because of the relatively warm temperatures, much of the ice had melted. Locals, said Prior, were concerned about them going out on the ice.

"When a Russian thinks you’re crazy, you should probably review what you’re about to do."

And yet despite the difficult conditions and ridiculous problems with the Ural, Prior said he'd recommend the trip to anyone.

"You’d be amazed at all the different terrain you can experience on just one frozen lake and a few forest tracks back onto the main road," said Prior. "It really was one of the best things I have done in my life."

"If I hadn’t [gone to Lake Baikal] I would have always regretted it," said Prior, "and looked back and thought 'What if…' or 'I wonder what that would have been like.'"

Now he knows.
