    After Yangtze Ship Tragedy, A Deleted Report Drives Speculation

    • Article
    • Comments (3)

    • accident

    • Censorship

    • Eastern Star

    • Politics

    • Transportation

    • Yangtze

      Rescuers work to raise the capsized ship in the Yangtze River in Jingzhou, China.
      Getty Images

      The disappearance of a Chinese newspaper report on the ship that sank in the Yangtze River with hundreds aboard last week has added to speculation over what caused China’s worst nautical disaster in nearly seven decades.

      The report in the Beijing News, a leading commercial newspaper in the Chinese capital, said the Eastern Star had received retrofits that made the vessel less safe. “Changes to the ship’s structure and additions to its length made it less stable, and increased the risk of capsize,” the report said, citing company officials and ship inspectors that it didn’t name. It also alleged that the ship’s owner maintained “special” ties with local shipping inspectors, particularly during the periods when the vessel was constructed and retrofitted.

      The article’s allegations couldn’t be independently verified. Officials investigating the sinking last week said the ship underwent retrofitting in 1997 and 2008. In both cases the vessel conformed with technical regulations, officials said, but they also said that authorities were doing more checks into the ship’s history.

      Advertisement

      Web-based versions of the Beijing News report, which also appeared in print, became inaccessible by late Monday morning. The article was still accessible via the newspaper’s official account on the WeChat mobile-messaging service as of Tuesday morning, as were copies of the report reproduced by other Chinese news media. The English-language edition of the Global Times tabloid, which is affiliated with the official Communist Party mouthpiece People’s Daily, also cited the Beijing News’ allegations in a Tuesday report.

      Three people at the Beijing News said that the newspaper took down online versions of the report. “The report put heavy pressure on certain parties. [The pressure] had nothing to do with the quality of the reporting,” said one of the people, who declined to name the parties.

      The newspaper’s editor in chief didn’t respond to requests for comment.

      The Eastern Star capsized in stormy Yangtze waters on June 1. Only 14 of the 456 people aboard are known to have survived the disaster—the official death toll reached 434 people as of Tuesday, while eight people remained unaccounted for.

      Chinese authorities have commandeered the flow of public information related to the disaster, such as by directing domestic media to rely on reports from state broadcaster CCTV and the official Xinhua News Agency, which have sought to portray government leaders favorably and curb speculation. Apart from the Beijing News article, observers say other publications have also removed reports that discussed possible causes of the sinking.

      Officials have cited bad weather as a factor and pledged a thorough probe into the accident. Media and public conjecture, meanwhile, have focused on issues such as the captain’s handling of the ship, possible lapses in weather warnings and the retrofitting, among others. None of ship’s crew or its owners have been accused of wrongdoing.

      According to the Beijing News, changes made to the Eastern Star in 1997—when it was converted from a ferry to a cruise ship—may have made it more susceptible to capsizing, and increased the difficulty for passengers to escape during emergencies.

      The changes included removing metal bed frames that were affixed to the deck, and replacing them with wooden bed frames that weren’t secured in position, the report said, citing a person it didn’t name who was with the company that carried out the retrofitting work.

      This change may have made the ship less safe, because loose bed frames can slide across the deck and shift the vessel’s center of gravity and block passengers’ escape routes, the Beijing News article said. According to an account reported by Xinhua, one survivor’s roommate became trapped when the ship’s tilt caused her bed to slide across the cabin and press her against the wall.

      Beijing News also cited the removal of certain cabin doors as a safety risk. Passenger cabins originally had two doors each—one facing either side of the vessel and the other facing an inner corridor—but the retrofitting removed the former and made escape more difficult, the newspaper said, citing the person.

      The 1997 retrofit was handled by the stated-owned Chongqing Shipyard, which declared bankruptcy in 2005, according to the report. Chongqing Eastern Shipping Corp.—the company that owns the Eastern Star—and the Chongqing Administration of Navigational Affairs, the city’s maritime regulator, didn’t respond to faxed requests for comment.

      The article’s allegations prompted social-media debate over the possible role that retrofitting works had played in the disaster. “Profit interest drives these conscienceless businessmen,” one user wrote on the newspaper’s official Weibo microblog. “We should look into the responsibility of those behind the decision and the design,” another user wrote.

      Others, however, cast doubt on whether the retrofitting was as significant as the Beijing News suggested. “Does it mean if this retrofitting wasn’t done, then the ship could have withstood force 12 winds?” one user asked, citing a scale of wind strength. “If it was a sudden event, what good would it have been if escape had been easier?” another said.
