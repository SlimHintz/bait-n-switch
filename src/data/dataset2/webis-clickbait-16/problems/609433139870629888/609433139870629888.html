Small-Business Owners Split on Raising Minimum Wage

  • Article
  • Comments (14)

  • Minimum wage

  • minimum wage law

  • Wages

  • By
  • Adam Janofsky
  • and
  • Leslie Josephs
Ryan Valachovic delivers an order of tater tots to the serving window at King David Dogs in Indianapolis on Wednesday. The Indianapolis-based hot-dog restaurant’s 10 or so staffers start at $7.50 an hour, 25 cents above the federal minimum.
ANDREW HANCOCK FOR THE WALL STREET JOURNAL

Small-business owners across the U.S. and in a range of industries are evenly split in their opinion of raising the minimum wage, according a May survey of 728 small firms by The Wall Street Journal and Vistage International.

Although three in four small-business owners said they do not employ minimum-wage workers, about 49% of respondents said the federal minimum wage of $7.25 should be raised, while 49% disagreed.

On Wednesday, the Los Angeles City Council voted to increase the city’s minimum wage to $15 an hour by 2020, from $9 an hour. Delaware’s state minimum wage increased to $8.25 per hour June 1, from $7.75 per hour.

Some small-business owners are coping with the higher staffing costs by changing their business strategies to avoid hiring more workers.

But other business owners seek to increase worker pay, according to the survey, because they face competition for workers with large corporations offering raises, as well as the tightening labor market in the U.S.

About 7% of respondents said they feel pressured to raise wages because large chains have recently said they would. For example, Target Corp. announced in March that it would boost pay to at least $9 an hour for all its workers, following similar moves by Wal-Mart Stores Inc. and TJX Cos. And starting July 1, McDonald’s Corp. will pay at least $1 an hour more than the local minimum wage at company-owned restaurants.

But a bigger motivation for small businesses to increase wages may come from direct competitors. One in three small firms reported that they had lost workers due to higher wage offers by competitors or other businesses.

“Small firms have begun to experience a tightening labor market, and as a result, most firms plan to offer wage increases,” said Richard Curtin, a University of Michigan economist who analyzed the survey results. Indeed, more than 75% of small-business owners said they planned to raise wages this year, with most respondents saying they would raise wages by 5% or less.

Most small employers said they would give a $1 raise quickly if the employee maintained a good work record. Of the 180 business owners who employed workers making minimum wage and responded to the question, 142 said they would offer a $1 raise within one year. Only two firms said they would never move the employee beyond the minimum wage.

Overall, about 15% of small-business owners said more than half of their workers earned the minimum wage. Seven percent reported that less than a tenth of their employees were paid minimum wage.

The survey was fielded online from May 11 to May 20, and is limited to the heads of companies with annual revenue ranging from $1 million to $20 million. See the full results for May.
