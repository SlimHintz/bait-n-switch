          W2W4: Lightning vs. Blackhawks, Game 3

          CHICAGO -- If you were to give overall grades through the first two games of what has become a rollicking Stanley Cup finals, you’d probably give the edge to the Tampa Bay Lightning.

          Tampa was good for much of the first 40 minutes of Game 1 before fading in a 2-1 loss, and then resilient in bouncing back in the face of a number of obstacles in Game 2 to even the series with a 4-3 win. Does that mean anything? With Games 3 and 4 set for Monday and Wednesday in Chicago, we’ll find out whether the Bolts can generate anything close to momentum in this series, or if we're in for a protracted punch-and-counterpunch situation. Here are some things to watch for in Game 3.

          Whither Ben Bishop? The central story line heading into Game 3 is the status of Lightning netminder Ben Bishop, who left Game 2 briefly in the third period, returned to the net, and then left for good a few moments later. Backup Andrei Vasilevskiy turned aside all five shots he faced, including a couple of good Chicago looks during a power play, to preserve the Lightning’s crucial 4-3 victory and earn his first-ever playoff victory.

          Bishop did skate in the traditional starter’s net at the morning skate, which would indicate that whatever was ailing him (believed to be connected to dehydration) in Game 2 has been remedied. But head coach Jon Cooper would not commit to a starter in spite of a number of questions skirting the issue.

          “How many more ways are we going to ask this?” Cooper asked. “I don’t know who’s starting. I’ll find out after this media session. We have two capable goalies that we have 100 percent confidence in who’s going to play. That’s all I can say. One of them’s playing, it’s Vasy or Bish. That’ll be determined tonight.”

          Bishop did meet with the media, but basically had nothing to add to the situation other than to say, “We’ll see” when asked if he felt good enough to play Monday. At one point Bishop said, “I feel like Marshawn Lynch right now,” a nod to the Seattle Seahawks running back whose interaction with the media sometimes devolves into repeated one-word answers.

          Bright lights, big series: There are lots of bright offensive stars in this series who have yet to cast much illumination on the outcome. The two captains -- Steven Stamkos of the Lightning and Jonathan Toews of the Blackhawks -- and former Conn Smythe Trophy winner Patrick Kane have combined for one assist through the first two games of the series, that a third-period assist from Toews on Brent Seabrook's goal that briefly tied Game 2 at 3-3.

          “We haven't found a way, myself personally, to get on the board yet,” Stamkos acknowledged before Game 3. “At this time of the year, it's about the wins. We've said that all along. This has been a group that's relied on our depth the entire season. We've seen it in the playoffs.

          “Our line in general has had some really good looks. Just haven't gone in.”

          Kane and Toews have also gone through stretches this spring where points have been hard to come by, but they showed late in the Western Conference finals why they’re considered two of the best "money" players in the game, just as Stamkos and his linemates were solid late in their seven-game showdown with the New York Rangers.

          The same is bound to happen this series. It’s just a question of which stars make their presence known first, and in a more impactful way. The other thing to factor into these next two games? The Blackhawks are 7-1 at home while the Lightning are 7-3 on the road. In short, something’s got to give, and look to the big guns to do the giving.

          Matchup made in heaven: Some lineup changes, both in terms of personnel and combinations, look to be in the offing for Chicago in Game 3. As expected, Kane dropped back to his normal line with Brad Richards and winger Bryan Bickell, who missed the first two games with what he said Monday was vertigo.

          “Tough playing when you’re dizzy,” Bickell said. “I feel 100 percent ready to come back into the lineup."

          Kris Versteeg would be the logical choice to come out of the lineup. Look for Marian Hossa moved back to the Hawks’ top line with Toews and Brandon Saad.

          The top Tampa defensive duo of Anton Stralman and Victor Hedman has played mostly against the Toews unit (which included Kane for long stretches of the first two games), but it will be interesting to see if Quenneville will try to get his top line out against the second pairing of Braydon Coburn and Jason Garrison now that he has the last change.

          By moving Kane down to the second line, and with the Blackhawks’ dynamic third line of Antoine Vermette, Teuvo Teravainen and Patrick Sharp very active in recent games, it’s going to put pressure on the Bolts’ defensive depth. The team’s third pairing of Andrej Sustr and Matt Carle have been prone to turnovers and mistakes in their own zone, and that could spell problems for the Bolts in games at the United Center.

          Quenneville also said Monday that defenseman Trevor van Riemsdyk might play as well. Not sure what the fascination is with a young defenseman who has zero Stanley Cup playoff experience and has not played since Nov. 16, but that Quenneville is considering giving the 23-year-old a chance reflects two things: the coach’s affection for van Riemsdyk’s poise and skill set, and his lack of confidence in Kyle Cumiskey (who played just 5:08 in Game 2), David Rundblad (7:48 in Game 2) and Kimmo Timonen (healthy scratch). It’s expected Rundblad, who skated longer Monday morning, would come out for van Riemsdyk.

          Crawford bounce-back? As soon as we sung the praises of Chicago netminder Corey Crawford in our W2W4 leading into Game 2, Crawford went and gave up four goals on 24 shots in the Game 2 loss. Two of the goals were deflections, but he didn’t look good on Tyler Johnson's short-side goal that put Tampa up 3-2 in the second period.

          When Quenneville was asked about Crawford after the game he said he was “just OK,” which in coach-speak is akin to saying "stink, stank, stunk." In truth, Crawford wasn’t that bad, but if the Bolts’ goaltending situation is a bit fluid then so, too, is Chicago’s. Crawford will have to be better.

          It’s hard to imagine a situation at this stage in which Quenneville would go to backup Scott Darling as he did early in the playoffs against the Nashville Predators, but you can never say never during the playoffs.
