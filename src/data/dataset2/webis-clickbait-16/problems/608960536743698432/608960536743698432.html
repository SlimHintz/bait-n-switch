15 qualities to look for when hiring a front-end developer

1.3k
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Scott Gerber2015-06-11 11:15:29 UTC

Front-end developers have to bring a range of talent to the table, from a knack for technical detail to a broad understanding of user experience and design. Simply glancing at a portfolio isn't going to cut it for a key front-end developer hire — you have to evaluate both their technical skill and their ability to communicate well with different stakeholders, from designers to your executive team.

For further insight, I asked 15 entrepreneurs from Young Entrepreneur Council (YEC) what qualities they focus on when interviewing potential candidates. Their answers are below.

See also: 15+ Great Ways to Find Web Design and Development Work

  1. A visual aesthetic that matches my brand

    2. First and foremost, I need someone whose visual aesthetic matches my brand, period. That's pretty much the first thing I look for when I recruit front-end developers. However, being detail-oriented is an absolute must. I don't just mean following my tasks to a T, I mean the kind of know-how that forces them to test for cross-platform inconsistencies without me asking. Does it look the same on your iPhone as it does on your MacBook? Screenshot that for me and troubleshoot before you present me with your final product. I've had front-end developers who have forgotten what seems like a tiny, innocuous piece of the puzzle but in the end told me they don't care about the tiny details informing the big picture.

    — Rob Fulton, Exponential Black

  2. Industry trend awareness and balance

    3. What are the latest trends in design and UI? Are they able to balance what suits your project with what suits their conscience? There's nothing more frustrating than spending money on something that will be out-of-touch and outdated in a matter of months. A front-end developer who is keenly aware of consumer trends, as well as design trends is someone who will be able to take whatever design brief they're given and spin it into something that I have never seen before. Finding someone with that quality is like trying to find a needle in a haystack. If they have a good eye for design, they're usually stuck in a particular way of doing things. I try to find someone who knows what's going on in my industry, what my customers will find most intuitive, and what looks good to both of us.

    — Cody McLain, SupportNinja

  3. A beautiful portfolio

    4. A good front-end developer is the type of person who has an incredible eye for detail and user experience. They also need to be the type of person who knows and interfaces with designers or can do some design work themselves. The best quality, though, is their ability to put it all together and make something happen. When looking for a front-end developer, I look at how well they have put together their own online portfolio. This is something nobody paid them for, but they did in order to showcase their skills. It is a great testament to how hard they work and what type of resources and skills they can readily tap to get things done.

    — Anthony Scherba, Yeti

  4. Up-to-date knowledge

    5. It's surprising how many developers I find who are just OK and don't have a GitHub account, and how many GREAT developers I find who do. The great people are in the communities sharing, chatting and showing their work. I've never found a great developer who has not had a GitHub or Dribbble account. If they don't have these accounts then, in my opinion, they're not up to speed.

    — Chris Brisson, Call Loop

    See also: HOW TO: Be a Hybrid Designer/Developer

  5. A specialization in your platform

    6. Do they specialize in developing for whatever back end or platform software I am using? I don’t choose someone who is a one-stop-shop because they may be generalists trying to learn how to do everything. Specialists get things done quickly and usually without error. Generalists can at times make mistakes, need revisions and ask too many questions.

    — Kevin Henrikson, Acompli (now Outlook iOS/Android @ Microsoft)

  6. Great communication

    7. A front-end developer is required to communicate a lot with designers. If they do not communicate regularly, they will end up misinterpreting the designs and incorrectly implementing them. A key challenge with front-end developers who try to quickly implement designs or work remotely with designers is that they may not ask questions about the details, and therefore make their own assumptions without consulting the designer. This leads to wasted time and frustrates both the designer and the developer. The key is that the front-end developer communicates clearly and asks a lot of questions.

    — Randy Rayess, VenturePact

  7. Previous success in the same field

    8. Find a front-end developer who has created a successful and functioning website with a company that is similar to yours and currently achieving on a level you would like to eventually reach. By doing this, you inevitably end up with a developer who can use the mistakes they think they may have made on the other company’s site to improve upon any design your site gets. You are getting the 2.0 version of a successful thing for the same price as the 1.0 version. That’s a deal.

    — Kumar Arora, Aroridex, Ltd.

  8. Strong awareness of developments in technology

    9. When looking for a great front-end developer, we want to see that they stay up to date and pay close attention to tech news and innovation. Not only does keeping up with development and web tech news show that they are truly passionate and curious about their position, but it also allows our company and development to keep pushing forward. It is important to stay on trend and ahead of the curve, so a front-end developer making suggestions and showing their research is incredibly impressive and appreciated!

    — Miles Jennings, Recruiter.com

    See also: 11 Outstanding Online Resources for Web Developers

  9. The ability to adapt

    10. With the rise of front-end frameworks such as Ember and Angular, the skill sets front-end developers need increasingly look like the skill sets that all developers need. Front-end developers face many of the same challenges (from MVC to modeling REST APIs) faced by back-end developers. The most important qualities are the ability to learn quickly and the ability to communicate clearly. Front-end frameworks are evolving and changing at a rapid pace, so being able to adapt quickly and work well with others is more important than knowing any particular technology.

    — Vivek Sharma, Movable Ink

  10. Creative technical proficiency

    11. A front-end developer must have technical proficiency to be able to translate designs into functional user interfaces and creativity to be able to develop beautiful, practical and user-friendly user interfaces. Without the creative mindset, a developer cannot be expected to produce aesthetically pleasing and user-friendly interfaces. And without technical mastery of the platform, language and frameworks, the developer will not be able to produce a user interface that performs well and is practical.

    — Jyot Singh, RTS Labs

  11. Critical thinking style

    12. In recruiting developers, including front-end devs, we’ve found that strong critical thinking skills are more predictive of high performance than the candidate's existing level of coding or design experience. We present our candidates with several types of problems that they’re challenged to work out in front of us. This allows us to evaluate their thinking style and ability more effectively than by simply asking them what their experience has been with X code language or X design software. It’s important to find front-end developers who demonstrate the ability to deconstruct a problem into its base components and figure out the most effective way to solve it. Attention to detail is another key component we look for during this process.

    — Zach Robbins, Leadnomics

  12. Vision

    13. A lot of front-end developer interviews focus on the portfolio of work. However, as any one in a creative position knows, a lot of what you've done professionally has been severely limited by the client's own limitations. For this reason, portfolio-focused interviews often uncover the candidates with the most interesting clients, not with the best skills.

    The one thing I look for above all else is vision. If you can describe the vision and the high-level goals to a designer and then have the designer come back with viable, on-target options, they are good at what they do. Handholding and spelling out exactly what should be built down to each pixel is not a good use of your time or skills.

    — Brennan White, Cortex

    See also: 10 Free Services to Monitor Your Site's Uptime

  13. Self-direction

    14. A great front-end developer is self-directed and ideally "gets" people. The front end of a product runs the interface (UI) that translates the back end of our software into what the world sees. Intuiting what users might and might not expect to see is super helpful in these situations. So often I encounter developers/designers who simply do exactly what they are told, to the letter. This isn't nearly as helpful as engineers who can conceptualize the goals of the story or feature that's being pushed and then make adjustments to achieve that goal.

    — Cooper Harris, Klickly

  14. UX/UI sensibility

    15. Finding a front-end developer has always been a challenge because it's not just about their technical skill set. Assuming the person is a good engineer, the most critical aspect comes down to their UI/UX sensibility. It may seem counterintuitive because they're going to code your product, not design it, but without that sensibility you end up with something that may be technically correct but definitely not what you had in mind.

    A very good front-end developer will help you make your UI/UX better than you thought it could be. In the last five engineering teams I built, this is the position that took the longest to fill, but it was always worth it!

    — Philippe Clavel, Rabbit

  15. Patience

    16. Front-end developers are the magicians behind the curtain of your company's web experience, especially for users who visit your website on a mobile device. That makes this role a really important one (and one that's not very glamorous). Why? Because being a front-end developer means A LOT of cross-browser testing and bug fixing, especially for interactive sites on a mobile device. (Hint: Browsers on Android phones are notorious for quirks.) That's why your front-end developer needs to be really patient. Expecting them to rigorously test your website across different browsers and proactively fix any usability issues they encounter is part of the role. To properly do this, though, they need patience. Getting your website to function the same across different browsers is no easy task.

    — Brian David Crane, Caller Smart Inc.
