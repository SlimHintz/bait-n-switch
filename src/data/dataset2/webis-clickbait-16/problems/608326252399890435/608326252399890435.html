England vs France, Women's World Cup: as it happened

Follow minute-by-minute commentary of England's opening game in the Women's World Cup against France on Tuesday, 9 June 2015

Up for the fight: England captain Steph Houghton says her side head into World Cup full of belief Photo: GETTY IMAGES

By Luke Brown

8:00PM BST 09 Jun 2015

• Maternity disputes, double lives and a bid for World Cup glory
• Is this the tournament that could revolutionise women's football?
• How Women's World Cup: ten stars to watch

20.00

And so ends our live coverage of England's first game of the 2015 World Cup, this evening. Although far from a vintage performance, Mark Sampson will take heart from the manner in which England dug in and defended, and they managed to restrict this talented French team to very few clear cut opportunities, which is a real achievement. They offered little up front of course, and the change from a 4-1-4-1 to a more expansive 4-3-3 didn't really come off, but they will still expect to qualify from their group and have certainly proved they can mix it with the bigger European teams.

19.55.

Perhaps more worrying for England than that narrow defeat, which I think the squad expected, is the sight of Eniola Aluko leaving the pitch in tears. She picked up a heavy knock in the first half of tonight's game, of course, and England will now sweat over her fitness for their remaining two group games.

Full time: France 1-0 England

Better from England in the second half, but France looked good for the three points, even if they were prevented from finding their top gear.

90 mins

France have the ball and they're not going to give it away needlessly. Renard in particular is in imperious form at the back, and her side look very comfortable as they hold this late England surge at arm’s length.

And then suddenly Kirby has a sight of goal! The ball breaks to her out of nowhere, but just as she's readying to pull the trigger, Georges recovers and puts in a wonderful saving tackle. And I think that could be it...

89 mins

Georges rather generously boots an aimless England long ball out of play for a throw in, which gives Eluko the chance to run at the French defence, but even she is unable to fashion anything.

She then receives the ball from Williams and exchanges passes with both Duggan and Kirby, but snatches at her half chance and blazes a shot over. That could well be England's final chance.

86 mins

Finally a chance as England win a throw on down by the French corner flag. The ball is worked for Bronze to swing a cross in from the left, but she overhits her pass and Bouhaddi claims the ball easily. Time is running out.

84 mins

What do England do?! They've barely touched the ball for nearly fifteen minutes now. Perhaps this is there chance, Bussaglia lashes a shot wide and England desperately need to retain the ball from this goal kick.

80 mins

France have shut England out of this game for the past ten minutes, and they look to have little interest in killing the game off by scoring a second. They withdraw the dangerous Le Sommer, and continue playing the ball around the midfield. England are hassling and harrying, but aren't offering anything going forward as of yet. They've reverted to a midfield diamond but the French are exploiting space down the side of the pitch very well.

76 mins

With fifteen minutes left, England make their final change: Moore replaces Chapman. They retain their 4-3-3 but honestly haven't had a sniff of the ball since reverting to this system. The French are patiently moving the ball around and seeking to feed their wingers at every opportunity.

73 mins

The introduction of Kirby was a very bold move, but England haven't really seen the ball since she came on. Le Sommer- who else- then goes close with a stinging thirty yard strike that flashes wide across Bardsley.

68 mins

Bold from Sampson: the young striker Fran Kirby is introduced, and England have switched to a 4-3-3. Alex Scott makes way.

There's then some ping-pong in the English box as two defenders hit the deck, before the ball hits the prone Doughan, fortunately blocking the attempt away from goal.

It also appears that Laura Bassett is elbowed in the face by Abily, as the defender goes down clutching her face. The referee missed that but the replays certainly appear damning.

66 mins

On the whole, France are playing within themselves, but Le Sommer has had a good game. Again she's fed the ball deep in the England half before driving onwards, and Chapman trips her for the first yellow of the game.

Necib takes the subsequent free kick but the French are unable to get a touch on it and it's watched harmlessly out of play.

61 mins

Sampson blinks first: forward Toni Duggan replaces Ellen White, and joins Eluko up front. She nearly helps to make an immediate impact as England build up a head of steam, but the final ball from out wide is once again poor from the Lionesses.

58 mins

Jill Scott goes close for England! Aluko wins a corner after some clever pressing off the ball, and the low corner is worked smartly to Scott. She meets the ball on the half-volley, and elects to steer it towards goal with the side of her boot rather than lash out at it. Unfortunately for England, it's more or less straight at Bouhaddi who collects it cleanly.

Scott also gets a boot on the leg for her troubles, and is briefly withdrawn for some treatment. She looks like she'll be okay though.

55 mins

Sampson has definitely given his players license to push forward this half. England aren't playing as deep and the service forward for Aluko has been better, but as a consequence Le Sommer is looking very dangerous. Rafferty steps off her momentarily and some space opens up, but her shot is wild and off target.

53 mins

France play a long ball which Thomis is about to run onto, but Bardsley times her charge out from her goal line to clear the ball just in time.

50 mins

Eluko has the ball on the edge of the box for England and briefly, briefly, threatens to repeat that goal against Holland, but her touch round Henry is heavy and France can clear. She's not being forced to lead the line by herself anymore, though, and the lessening of that burden will hopefully give her more of a chance to express herself.

46 mins

Le Sommer has a chance to make it 2-0, but her drive is straight at Bardsley. She struck the ball well and England are lucky that her targeting was off. England are committing more players now, and that's going to afford the French chances.

45 mins

And the second half is underway.

18:53

Remember, if you have any thoughts on that 45 minutes of action, or perhaps lack of it, you can get in contact with me at luke.brown@telegraph.co.uk.

Half time: France 1-0 England

And that's half-time, England will be rather pleased to only be 1-0 down at this point, I think. The French really do look a good, aggressive team, even if they're not playing to the uttermost of their ability, and pose a major threat whenever their wing backs overlap with the wide midfielders. But England, sitting deep and playing with an extraordinary level of discipline, are keeping things tight and haven't made too many mistakes at the back.

But they have absolutely no hope at the other end, playing this deep. And I've heard that the odd goal comes in fairly useful in this sport. Sampson needs to make a change.

45 mins

England clinging on at the end of this first half. First Hourara is failed on the edge of the box by Rafferty, but the referee waves play on. Then Thiney passes when she perhaps should have shot, before Boulleau's dangerous cross is well claimed by Bardsley.

40 mins

A shot for England! And it wasn't a bad one at that. This time it's Bouhaddi who flaps at the ball, and Chapman receives the ball before improvising a lob in limited space. It goes wide, but proof, perhaps, that the French backline is fallible.

38 mins

Phew, England let off the hook there. First Bardsley, who really looks to be struggling in these conditions, flaps at a corner. Thiney is given the ball from the subsequent attack and works it onto her left foot, but she thrashes a powerful shot narrowly wide.

37 mins

Unlucky from Bronze! She breaks forward with the ball and looks to have the better of Georges, but the defender does just enough to shoulder her off the ball. The pitch looked to have opened up, then. It doesn't take long for France to regain their shape and Thiney attempts to swing a cross into the box, but Rafferty manages to block.

35 mins

Bronze looks a tidy player but she's just operating too deep down that left hand side to really inflict any damage. England are still playing with a very defensive mentality and aren't really inflicting any pressure on the ball at present, and an equaliser looks a long way off.

33 mins

England are rattled and France are keeping the ball very well. Alex Scott and Eluko manage to push forward and attempt to work a corner, but France see the ball out.

28 mins

GOAL! France 1-0 England (Le Sommer '29) But Bardsley has less chance with that! Le Sommer picks the ball up a similar distance away from Thiney just moments before, but puts her foot through the ball rather than attempting to place it. It's a great effort, and her shot is too quick for the England goalkeeper who appears to have her view blocked, and she dives too late. 1-0, and now England have to change their entire game plan.

listen to ‘France score to go 1-0 up against England’ on audioBoom

26 mins

Wow- Aluko breaks forward and plays it off Georges for a corner, but a goal kick is given. The conspiracy has started people! This has Sepp's moneyed hands all over it. Aluko does look threatening though and her direct running looks to be England's only chance of poaching a goal at present.

The French then break and Thiney strikes the ball sweetly from the edge of the srea, but Bardsley gets down well to save.

24 mins

Another good chance for the French and again Le Sommer is the danger, but her shot is deflected wide for a corner, which is then cleared.

21 mins

Williams chops down Le Sommer and is fortunate not to go into the book, but Necib only manages to smack it straight into the palms of Bardsley.

Worryingly, Eluko has just hobbled off the pitch for some treatment on her foot. England desperately can't afford to lose her, especially given her role as England's only attacking outlet.

19 mins

England appeal loudly for a backpass after Scott strides forward before playing a through ball, which clips off a French defender and into the hands of Bouhaddi. To me that looked like a deflection, nothing more.

While they're pestering the ref, the French move it forward and again seek to work it through to Thomis, but Rafferty does incredibly well to shield her off the ball, before winning a free kick to boot.

16 mins

Fantastic work from Jill Scott as she strides forward on the ball before winning a throw on deep into their half. Williams and Scott then exchange some neat passes on a rare foray forward, but her through ball travels straight through to the French keeper Bouhaddi, with Aluko dropping too deep seeking to contribute to the build up play.

Still- England were in the French half then! It's coming home, etc.

14 mins

Worrying for England: Thomis is played through for the French and looks to have slipped past her marker, but the referee blows his whistle for a push. If she hadn't, Thomis would have been through and in a very dangerous position.

England are meanwhile playing incredibly deep, as this screenshot shows. All 11 women are currently firmly camped in their own half.

10 mins

Well it has been a tight, tactical opening ten minutes, and France have absolutely dominated since that England free-kick within the first two or three minutes. Houara and Abily have both looked very dangerous from wide positions.

8 mins

Abily's header flashes wide in the first real moment of danger for England. England are going to have to work hard to stop crosses coming into the box because the French have a real height advantage, as well as the strikers to capitalise on any mistakes.

5 mins

This is a good start from England, who are playing deep but look keen to work the counter. France have started to come back into the game, however, and are unlucky when Boulleau's powerful drive is deflected wide for a corner.

4 mins

First chance for England as Fara Williams whips in a free kick from deep within the French half but Jill Scott is unable to get her head onto it. England maintain the pressure though and win a corner, which is wasted.

It's started absolutely chucking it down by the way and, coupled with the driving winds, conditions are hardly perfect right now. England have another corner though.

1 min

Kick-off and the French, attacking from left to right, pour forward at the first opportunity, but England clear the danger. Aluko then makes a quick break forward but there's no English player anywhere near to support her run.

17.59

Sampson has gone for a relatively conservative line-up, and you can't blame him given the French attacking threat. Lianne Sanderson drops to the bench to enable England to play a 4-1-4-1, with Lucy Bronze coming into the team to act as a defence orientated wing back.

17.55

Only five minutes until England kick off their World Cup campaign! Quick, get the bunting out, attach those little St George's flags to the top of your car and blast out your England anthem of choice.

And whilst we're all waiting, let's all marvel at this rather incredible picture of two of the England team hard at work ahead of this crunch fixture.

17.50

Ten minutes to go, which means there's just enough time to brush up on your history.

This will be England's fourth World Cup, and- true to the English way- the team have never got past the quarter-finals. It's in our DNA.

Last time out, the team did fantastically well in the group stages, starting their campaign with a point against Mexico before a hard fought 2-1 win over New Zealand. The much fancied Japan were next, and England put in a memorable performance to win 2-0, and finish top of the group, thus ensuring an easier quarter-final draw.

Except, the quarter-final draw wasn't that easy at all. England had to play the runners-up of Group A, which were France, after they lost 4-2 in the group stages of the competition to Germany. And in the subsequent showdown, oh so predictably, France progressed to the semi-finals at England's expense after Faye White missed her crucial penalty.

A quarter-final exit and a penalty shootout defeat. Two scores in England World Cup bingo in one fell swoop.

17.40

The 2015 World Cup is a new experience for England manager Mark Sampson, who took the reins after Hope Powell's 15 years in charge of the team were brought to an end following England's disappointing group stage exit from Euro 2013. Sampson, only 32, previously worked under Roberto Martinez at Swansea City, before becoming manager of the Bristol Academy Women's Club in 2009.

Sampson's England record certainly isn't to be sniffed at: he's tasted defeat only four times and currently enjoys an impressive win rate of 70%. That's a full 25% better than Roy Hogson, for the record. Plus, to date, Sampson hasn't presided over a match that a terrestrial television channel has actually had to publicly apologise for.

Sampson was, however, left downhearted after England's crushing 3-0 friendly defeat to Germany at the end of last year, however, and has admitted to making tactical changes since that game. He'll be hoping they prove effective against France, who are just two places below them in the FIFA women's world rankings. Here he is assessing England's chances ahead of the tournament.

17.30

If you haven't already, make sure you check out Mick Brown's brilliant article from the English training camp, shortly before the team departed for Montreal.

In early April the squad were to be found at St George"s Park, the FA's national football centre in Burton-upon-Trent, preparing for a friendly against China. On one of St George's 12 training pitches, half the England squad were being put through a series of fitness, body-strengthening and tactical exercises.

Even in these limited exercises certain players caught the eye: the Arsenal full-back Alex Scott, who demonstrated an impressive turn of pace and a terrific cross; Fran Kirby, the Reading forward, who looked cool under pressure and deft on the ball; and the Arsenal striker Lianne Sanderson, a tall, powerfully built figure with a blond mohawk and a finish equally lethal with head or foot.

At the end of the session, the group came together in a huddle, breaking out with a cry of "1-2-3 TEAM!" When The Telegraph photographer asked to take pictures, Sanderson jokingly protested. "I haven't got any make-up on!" Cristiano Ronaldo knows exactly how you feel.

17.20

But England have some talented players of their own. Perhaps the most exciting at present is the 28 year old striker Eniola Aluko. She has 32 goals in 90 games for her country, and benefited greatly from successful stints with three different clubs in America. She returned to these shores when she signed for Birmingham in 2012, and shortly after that move she was off again, bought by Chelsea.

Aluko is something of a stalwart of the England set up, and memorably played in Euro 2005 whilst also popping back home to take her A Levels. Fortunately there'll be no such distractions at this World Cup, and with any luck she might replicate some of this kind of form:

France are ranked as the third best team in the world and have a number of dangerous players who have the ability to hurt England. Perhaps chief amongst those is the Paris Saint Germain forward, Marie-Laure Delie.

Delle averages more than a goal every game with PSG in the Division 1 Fminine, with a formidable record of 38 in 37. She is currently the leading goalscorer in the national team, too, with 57 goals in 85 games at international level. The French are an attacking, aggressive team, and Delle will be on the end of the vast majority of their forward play. Alex Scott and Laura Bassett are going to have their hands full attempting to contain her.

France also have a very talented youngster on the bench by the name of Griedge Mbock Bathy. So talented, in fact, that she made this rather brilliant list of 10 stars to watch at the Women's World Cup, which was written by this cracking little publication you should totally check out (hint: it's us).

17.00

The French are better than the English at many things. Revolutions. Making cheese. French GCSEs. And, until this moment, women's football. Not a single player this evening was even alive the last time England beat Les Blues 41 long years ago, and it was France who kicked the Three Lionesses out of the 2011 World Cup, on penalties (predictably).

However, tonight is the night things change. England look good. They're up five places in the world rankings, to sixth, and have changed their manager since the 2011 World Cup. England have also progressed from a band of part-time players to an elite squad of full-time professionals, amassing over 1,200 international caps between them.

There's a confidence surrounding the England camp, and the squad knows that a win against France here tonight would be a statement of intent. The tide is turning. The stars have aligned. The writing is on the wall, in font 72 Helvetica. England have never been in better shape, and everybody knows it.

So don't be surprised if the French win 4-0.

And remember, if you want to share your thoughts on either the game or other things France are better than England at, you can get in contact with me at luke.brown@telegraph.co.uk.

13.00

ENGLAND v FRANCE (kick-off 6pm)

Captain Steph Houghton says England have the belief to win the Women's World Cup in Canada this summer.

England take on France in their opening Group F clash and Manchester City's Houghton is confident the Lionesses have what it takes to go all the way in the showpiece.

"We can't come to a tournament and not believe," she said. "We know that we've prepared fully and worked hard and we believe and trust in the game plan the manager's given us. If we stay together....there's no doubt we've got some players that have got world-class ability and it's all about performance.

"As a captain of this side, I fully believe we have the players to potentially go all the way.

"We know we have some world-class opposition to face and we've got to concentrate on one game at a time and that's France tomorrow, who are a team we have to respect.

"They've been at the top of their game for the last three or four years."
