    Rick Perry thinks America needs a president who’s served in the military. Will voters agree?

    Liz Goodwin
    Senior National Affairs Reporter
    June 4, 2015

    Former Texas Gov. Rick Perry speaks to supporters before announcing his 2016 presidential run on Thursday in Dallas. (Photo: Tim Sharp/AP)

    ADDISON, Texas — “Imagine a president who’s worn the uniform and knows what it’s like to serve,” retired Navy SEAL Pete Scobell told a crowd of Rick Perry supporters Thursday as they waited for the former Texas governor to announce his second run for the presidency in a stifling hot airplane hangar.

    Several in the crowd hooted “yes” and “uh-huh,” nodding in agreement.

    Once Perry, in a dark suit with his now trademark thick-framed glasses, took the stage, he encouraged his supporters to keep imagining that president who had served. He announced his candidacy flanked by at least nine veterans, many of them decorated war heroes. Behind them stood a giant C-130 cargo plane — the kind of aircraft Perry flew during his six-year peacetime stint in the Air Force during the 1970s.

    “I was proud to wear the uniform of our country as an Air Force officer and aircraft commander,” Perry said of his time as a pilot in the military, when he participated in several drought and earthquake relief missions abroad. He later compared President George Washington to the military heroes who stood behind him.

    “George Washington lived in the service of a cause greater than self,” Perry said. “If anyone is wondering if America still possesses the character of selfless heroes, I am here to say, ‘Yes, I am surrounded by such heroes.’”

    With his speech, Perry hearkened back to a time when almost all of the nation’s leaders had also fought in the military. Harry S. Truman, Ulysses S. Grant and William Henry Harrison were rocketed to the White House by their military prowess. And more modern presidents, such as Ronald Reagan and George H.W. Bush, served in World War II.

    But more recently, military service has not translated into electoral success. As U.S. Naval Academy professor John Nagl writes, the candidate with the least distinguished military record or no record at all has won every election since 1992 — from George W. Bush to Barack Obama. This might partially reflect the divisiveness of more recent conflicts, particularly Vietnam. But it wasn’t until the last election, in 2012, that for the first time in modern history neither presidential candidate had served in the armed forces. This reflects larger population changes: Less than 0.5 percent of Americans serve in the military now, compared to more than 12 percent during World War II.

    Slideshow: Rick Perry through the years

    Among the presumptive 2016 GOP candidates, only Sen. Lindsey Graham and Perry have served in the armed forces. Perry’s piloting days set him apart in a crowded GOP field — and he’s emphasized them far more than when he was on the stump in 2012, when he tended to focus more on Texas’ booming economy.

    Still, Perry isn’t hoping to appeal only to voters who believe a former soldier makes for a better commander in chief. Emphasizing his military past is a way to claim foreign policy knowledge and experience — something the ex-governor is comparatively weak on next to some of his GOP rivals.

    “His experience serving in the Air Force is irreplaceable in terms of helping him have that [foreign policy] judgment,” said Perry senior adviser Avik Roy. “The judgment comes from having the human feel for what it means to put our soldiers and sailors and airmen on the line.”

    Perry hopes to use this foreign policy credential to attack Obama for the persistent spread of the Islamic State in areas in Iraq once secured by U.S. troops, and for nuclear negotiations with Iran. But this strategy also draws him away from a potentially winning message about Texas’ economic performance under his governorship and into territory where he has less expertise.

    “The danger of focusing on national security, military affairs, foreign policy is pretty soon, someone’s going to ask you who are the Shia and who are the Sunnis,” said Cal Jillson, a political science professor at Southern Methodist University who’s tracked Perry’s political career.

    Perry has been boning up on both domestic and foreign policy for months in preparation for his run, and his advisers admit he was too “cocky” and unprepared during his first campaign. But one flub in an interview or debate will quickly remind voters of his infamous “oops” moment in November 2011 that served as the nail in the coffin of his candidacy. Perry could not remember the name of the third federal agency he vowed to eliminate as president during a Republican debate.

    While his foray into foreign policy is new, Perry’s interest in veterans once they return home has been a part of his political life for years. As governor, Perry supported legislation to give state tax breaks to veterans and encourage colleges to grant them credit for their military training and experience. He also created a VA Strike Force in 2012, providing an outside team of counselors to help the federal hospital system reduce wait times for medical care for Texas veterans. Perry has also highlighted his close relationship with “Lone Survivor” author and Navy SEAL Marcus Luttrell, who lived with the Perrys while suffering from a pain killer addiction after his deployment. Perry is godfather to Luttrell’s children, and the warrior is campaigning with the governor in South Carolina and New Hampshire next week.

    “The governor is deeply concerned about the way we treat our veterans after they return home and how ineffective the government has been in delivering health care to veterans,” Roy said.

    Retired Special Operations Officer Paul Craig, one of the veterans who flanked Perry during his announcement and a former member of Luttrell’s unit, said the former governor’s commitment to veterans is genuine. He recalled Perry helping an acquaintance in New York get medical care for his traumatic brain injury after he had trouble receiving treatment at the VA in his home state. 

    “A lot of people get that it’s politically expedient to get behind veterans, but that’s not Gov. Perry,” Craig said. The Navy veteran acknowledged that Perry came across as shaky and unprepared four years ago, but says he thinks this run is an entirely different story.

    “His initial run in ’12, he had other things going on,” Craig said, referencing Perry’s back problems. “I don’t think he was the man he is today.”

    Correction: An earlier version of this story incorrectly said John Tyler campaigned for the White House based on his military prowess. It was President William Henry Harrison, not Tyler, who campaigned on his victory at the Battle of Tippecanoe.
