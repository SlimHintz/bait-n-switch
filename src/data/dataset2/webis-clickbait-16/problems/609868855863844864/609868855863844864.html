‘The Wolfpack’ Tells How the Six Angulo Brothers Were Saved by the Movies

Siblings grew up in a New York City apartment—largely indoors, cut off from society

‘The Wolfpack,’ about six movie-obsessed brothers living in New York’s Lower East Side, won the grand prize for documentary at the Sundance Film Festival this year. Four of the brothers talk with The Wall Street Journal about how the film changed their lives and what comes next. Photo: Getty
By
Caryn James
June 10, 2015 9:25 p.m. ET
3 COMMENTS

Crystal Moselle’s “The Wolfpack,” which won the grand prize for documentary at the Sundance Film Festival this year, has been accumulating critical praise and attention for its subjects ever since.

In the verité-style film, a simple walk outdoors is recalled as a dramatic act of rebellion. On screen, Mukunda Angulo says he left his family’s apartment by himself for the first time when he was 15. It was 2010, and he had never walked the streets of his neighborhood on New York’s Lower East Side alone. One of six movie-obsessed brothers, he disguised his identity with a homemade mask modeled on Michael Myers’ in the “Halloween” series so no one would recognize him.

Soon he and his brothers, then 11 to 18, were leaving the apartment together. They were defying their father, who they say had kept them, along with their mother and older sister, in his protective thrall all their lives—largely indoors, cut off from society.

The siblings sometimes stayed in the apartment for months at a time, they say, but watched movies endlessly and re-enacted their favorites—everything from “The Lord of the Rings” to “Pulp Fiction”—complete with costumes and props and some dead-on acting. “The Wolfpack” lets them tell their story.

Ms. Moselle met them soon after their rebellion began. They gave her some home movies, which are seen in the documentary. She gave them a camera to shoot more and filmed them herself for more than four years. Vivid presences on screen, they recreate more movies and talk about their childhood and how they emerged into the world.

ENLARGE
Director Crystal Moselle on the set of ‘The Wolfpack’ Photo: Magnolia Pictures

As in most verité-style documentaries, the non-narrative approach leaves lingering questions and unverified facts. The director becomes an off-camera factor in some episodes, such as when the brothers are seen going to a movie theater for the first time and taking the subway to Coney Island. In a recent interview, four of the brothers repeated their accounts from the movie, which their mother verified.

Their mother, Susanne, an idealist from the Midwest, met her husband, Oscar, a Hare Krishna follower, while traveling in Peru. The brothers say he wanted a tribe of children, all with long hair and names from Hindu scriptures. In addition to Mukunda, the brothers are Bhagavan, Govinda, Narayana, Krsna and Jagadisa.

The siblings lived in a four-bedroom apartment in public housing and were home-schooled by their mother. They recall that their father instilled a fear of anything outside their walls, warning that the streets were filled with drugs, violence and other dangers.

“It’s not like they were chained,” Ms. Moselle says. “They were just not socializing with the outside world. They didn’t leave the house because they were scared”—both of society and of challenging their father.

Four of the Angulo Brothers from the documentary film, “The Wolfpack,” which won the grand prize for documentary at Sundance this year, reenact their favorite film characters.

Oscar Angulo hasn’t spoken publicly about the film, and when a Wall Street Journal reporter knocked on the family’s door Tuesday, Mukunda answered, saying his father had been out all day. Susanne later said via email: “Oscar has no comment at this point.”

Ms. Moselle said that soon after the brothers started leaving the house, she spotted a pack of adolescent boys with striking waist-length hair and dark glasses. “I chased them down the street,” she says.

They were wary until she said she was a filmmaker. She began helping them with their own short films, but it wasn’t until a year into the friendship that she learned of their bizarre family situation, and the documentary began taking shape.

Oscar appears in the film briefly, with subtitles under his heavily accented English. In one scene, he seems to defend himself by pointing out the neighborhood perils he hoped to protect his family from.

ENLARGE
The Angulo brothers: Narayana, Govinda, Jagadisa, Bhagavan, Mukunda and Krsna in ‘The Wolfpack’ Photo: Magnolia Pictures

“You don’t know how I felt here when I saw all those people here doing things,” he says. “It was a piece of jail outside.”

On screen, Susanne says of his control over the family: “There were probably more rules for me than there were for them,” meaning her children. Throughout the film, she is seen gradually coming into her own. (Their daughter, whom Susanne confirms has cognitive disabilities, is seen in the background.)

Mukunda says in the film that when he left the house in his “Halloween” mask that day, he was picked up by police, then sent for a short hospital stay. In an interview, Ms. Moselle says he was there less than a week. The brothers were in therapy for a couple of years after that. Child services and police spokesmen said privacy laws precluded them from confirming this information.

In the movie, the boys sneer at their therapy experience, but they also became more socialized.

“The Wolfpack” suggests that re-enacting movies was the boys’ lifeline. Now, they are starring in a movie that has changed their world. The four oldest—Bhagavan, now 23; Govinda and Narayana, fraternal twins who are 22; and Mukunda, 20—recently sat down to talk about their post-Sundance lives.

They are the same movie-obsessed brothers seen on-screen, but have emerged as confident, forward-looking young men. All four have jobs, and although only Govinda has moved out of the family apartment, the others hope to head out within the year.

Watch a film clip from the documentary “The Wolfpack.”

They are so set on careers in film, they are starting their own production company. They arrived for the interview in playful costumes, dressed to shoot a video for The Wall Street Journal, in which they performed their favorite lines from movies including “Saturday Night Fever” and “Taxi Driver”—in character. Bhagavan’s print shirt and medallion chain turned him into John Travolta from “Saturday Night Fever,” and Govinda’s army surplus jacket echoed Robert DeNiro in “Taxi Driver.”

As the documentary ends, Govinda is moving into his own place and Mukunda is working as a production assistant. Some of the brothers had cut their hair.

Now, a year later, Govinda says about being on his own, “I don’t know if liberating is the right word, but it’s definitely a big feeling of freedom.” He wants to be a cinematographer and makes a living as a freelance camera operator and assistant cameraman, and occasionally shoots and edits video.

“To be in this business in New York City was scary at first, but I’ve embraced it,” he says.

ENLARGE
Krsna, Jagadisa and Mukunda Angulo in ‘The Wolfpack’ Photo: Magnolia Pictures

Mukunda, who wants to direct, also works as a production assistant, as well as an art director’s assistant and prop master. The brothers are also writing and shooting original material. Govinda says, “We’re building a portfolio for our startup company,” which might be called Wolfpack Productions, and which Ms. Moselle is helping them with. Bhagavan and Narayana, who have more reserved personalities than their brothers do, also plan to be involved in the production company as well. In their day jobs, Bhagavan studies and is a corps dancer at the Hip Hop Dance Conservatory, and Narayana works on environmental issues for a nonprofit public-advocacy group.

Mukunkda says that the two youngest brothers, ages 18 and 16, who chose not to be interviewed, have changed their names and begun using their mother’s maiden name. “They wanted to start anew and feel fresh,” Mukunda says. Narayana says he is considering a name change too, “for the same reasons, to start again.”

As the movie shows Susanne breaking free of her husband’s hold, she talks on the phone to her own mother, whom viewers are told she hadn’t been in touch with for 20 years. That scene was shot by Mukunda, who says, “My mom calling her mom felt like it was going to be a big moment that should be caught on camera.”

Govinda says, “Mom is the hero of our story. She was the love of the family. She held everything together.”

In the documentary, the brothers sound unforgiving of their father. Although all of the family except Govinda lives together, the sons say the father’s role has changed. “We have our own space, he’s got his own space, there’s an understanding,” Mukunda says.

Govinda adds, “Now he’s along for the ride.” All four brothers smile at the idea of that reversal. Their mother, who was observing the interview, later said via email, “They are smiling at their triumph over his rule.”

—Mark Armao contributed to this article.
