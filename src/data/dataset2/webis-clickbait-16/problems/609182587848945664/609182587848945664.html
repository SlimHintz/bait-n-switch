Silicon Valley: Twitter CEO Dick Costolo had it tough

928
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By JP Mangalindan2015-06-12 02:05:04 UTC

Twitter sent shockwaves throughout Silicon Valley on Thursday when it announced that Dick Costolo was stepping down as CEO, and cofounder Jack Dorsey would fill the role as interim chief executive. Costolo's last day with the company is July 1, although he will remain on Twitter's board of directors.

See also: In shock move, Twitter founder Jack Dorsey takes over as interim CEO

For his part, Costolo explained the move was his call and that he initiated conversations with several board members about creating a CEO succession plan at the end of 2014. But it's hard to believe his departure wasn't influenced by pressure from investors, who punished Twitter's stock over more recent challenges like stagnant user growth, user retention and disappointing results from its direct response advertising business.

Many people in the tech industry maintained Costolo is a great leader who was instrumental in Twitter's rise. Under Costolo, the social network grew its user base into the hundreds of millions and built its earliest ad products.

"I think his departure reflects just how hard it can be to balance longer-term growth and innovation initiatives when you are constantly faced with the short-term expectations of public markets," explained Clara Shih, CEO of Hearsay Social and a Starbucks board member. "I have no doubt he'll find a new mission where his talent in building transformational companies will shine, and in the meantime, we all hope that Twitter is able to find a new leader who has the courage, conviction, and investor confidence to take the company from good to great.

Serial entrepreneur and tech investor Gina Bianchini, who runs the social network platform Mightybell, echoed Shih's sentiment.

“Dick is an outstanding leader. I’d love to see the hedge fund managers who called for his head run a company of Twitter’s size or influence," Bianchini contended. "This job is not easy. That being said, Jack has the moral authority to move the company forward."

With Dorsey back in the driver's seat, however, expect some tweaks here and there in Twitter's strategy, mostly having to do with the pace of innovation.

"Jack Dorsey will likely bring Twitter a renewed focus on product, and that is good for user growth, as long as the monetization initiatives are not getting lost," said Cyriac Roeding, CEO of mobile shopping app Shopkick.

"It looks like Chris Sacca's core message that product has to be front and center resonated," explained an active tech investor who declined to be named for privacy reasons. "Jack is a product visionary. Assuming Jack has got the time to dedicate to the role, my guess is you'll see product iteration and product release frequency dramatically increase. I'd also guess you'll see many of Chris' recommendations start to roll out."

Twitter under Jack was great. Twitter under Dick was great. Twitter under Jack will be great.

— Aaron Levie (@levie) June 11, 2015

Other folks in the tech community were less sold on Dorsey's return, which even Dorsey emphasized on Thursday was a temporary measure until Twitter finds a successor.

"Forcing the CEO out might make the street feel better for a few weeks, but it certainly won't drive user growth," argued Tim Chen, CEO of the personal finance site NerdWallet. "I'm pretty sure if Jack Dorsey had a silver bullet growth idea, he would have told Dick about it."

But perhaps a Tweet from Matthew Prince, CEO of Internet security startup Cloudflare, summed up some outsiders' sentiment best:

Twitter, Inc. puzzles me.

— Matthew Prince (@eastdakota) June 11, 2015
