Why it matters to keep the world's oceans pure and clean

4.1k
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Matt Petronzio2015-06-08 14:22:36 UTC

Oceans may contain 97% of the Earth's water, but climate change, overfishing and other human impacts have thrown their health into decline.

Only about 3% of the world's oceans are legally protected, yet we have proof that marine protected areas — where human activity is restricted — offer healthier ecosystems, increased biodiversity and even have the potential to increase gender equality in nearby communities, among other benefits.

See also: 7 important ocean trends, and what we can do about them

That's why international governments, organizations and individuals are stepping up, adding momentum to the movement to conserve increased volumes of Earth's oceans.

In honor of World Oceans Day on Monday, we put together a brief explainer of what marine protected areas are, why they're important and how the global community is working harder than ever to conserve huge swaths of the sea.

What are marine protected areas?

The true definition of what constitutes a marine protected area can vary, but there's at least one common thread: It's a designated part of a body of water — whether that means an ocean or a lake — that is essentially an ecological safe haven, often likened to national parks.

That means human activity — such as tourism, fishing and mineral extraction — are limited or altogether prohibited, monitored by law from local, state, territorial, native, regional or national authorities, according to the global conservation collaboration Protect Planet Ocean.

Because these authorities vary, marine protected areas can also include areas with other names: marine reserves, protected marine areas, no-take zones, ocean sanctuaries, etc. The term "marine protected area" is often used to provide a comprehensive, overarching description. (That goes to show, however, that not all marine protected areas are completely off-limits; some simply reduce the human traffic there.)

The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) formally defines a marine protected area as "a clearly defined geographical space, recognized, dedicated and managed, through legal or other effective means, to achieve the long-term conservation of nature with associated ecosystem services and cultural values."

While slightly vague, such a definition, the IUCN explains, prevents exploitative industries like industrial-scale fishing to claim they protect the ocean, while also allowing flexibility for governments to establish their own rules. All marine protected areas, however, must exhibit long-term goals of conservation and ocean recovery. Of course, the areas can have a number of more specific objectives, including maintaining biodiversity and protecting endangered species.

Why are marine protected areas important?

Marine protected areas provide a wealth of benefits to the ecosystems in which they're located. They help maintain and increase biodiversity — which is essential for the natural sustainability of our planet — and offer sanctuaries for endangered and commercial species. Fish populations can recover and reproduce, and in turn increase fish catches in nearby fishing grounds.

That's why scientists maintain that marine protected areas can actually help the fishing industry — in a sustainable, responsible way. A recent study commissioned by the World Wildlife Foundation found that increasing ocean protection could increase related jobs, resources and services, potentially resulting in net benefits of between $490 billion and $920 billion worldwide by 2050.

In the same study, marine protected areas in Fiji even showed that, because of nearby communities' economic and social ties to marine life, marine protected areas can help reduce poverty, enhance governance, and benefit human health and gender equality.

Furthermore, certain physical features of marine protected areas, such as coral reefs and mangrove forests, can act as barriers against natural disasters worsened by climate change — they're like natural breakwaters and shock-absorbers, according to the Marine Conservation Institute. Mangroves can dampen the size of hurricane-related storm surges, as can healthy and large areas of oyster beds.

Lastly, scientists can study marine protected areas to further measure the human influences on non-protected areas, addressing the latter's needs accordingly.

What has the world done so far?

Many countries have established specific targets and goals, and have participated in international agreements to increase ocean conservation. The Marine Conservation Institute has a pretty comprehensive list here.

Perhaps the most famous marine protected areas — or at least most recognized by the average person — are parts of Australia's Great Barrier Reef, the world's largest coral reef system. The marine protected areas within it were among the first, established in 1975.

Forty years later, studies are finding that coral in the Great Barrier Reef's no-take marine reserves were four times healthier than coral in non-protected areas.

Other notable marine protected areas include the Republic of Kiribati's Phoenix Islands Protected Area, Ecuador's Galapagos Marine Reserve and South Africa's Prince Edward Islands Marine Protected Area.

In 2000, the U.S. officially set up a national system of marine protected areas within the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), called the National Marine Protected Areas Center. This center is in charge of the planning and implementation of new marine protected areas around the country.

Statistics vary, but estimates over the past few years state that between 2% and 4% of the world's oceans are protected. Marine scientists estimate that 30% should be protected, for the good of the planet.

Who are the major players in new efforts?

Increased activism and awareness surrounding ocean conservation have galvanized governments to increase the number of marine protected areas around the world.

In September 2014, U.S. President Barack Obama announced an addition to the Pacific Remote Islands Marine National Monument, established in 2009, expanding it by 490,000 square miles — six times its original size. The goal of this area is to reduce the effects of climate change, overfishing and ocean acidification, and the expansion called international attention to the fight to save our seas. At the time, the U.S. roughly doubled the size of marine protected areas worldwide.

The UK also showed its dedication to the cause in March 2015, when the British government announced a special budget to create the world's largest single marine reserve, around the Pitcairn Islands in the Pacific, and it's expected to ban commercial fishing in that area.

Governments aren't the only ones taking action, though. Pew's Global Ocean Legacy project works extensively with communities, governments and scientists to protect ocean environments. Its recent work includes marine reserve efforts in waters near Chile's Easter Island, French Polynesia's Austral Islands and New Zealand's Kermadec region.

Mission Blue, the initiative led by pioneering marine biologist Sylvia Earle and her organization, the Sylvia Earle Alliance, is focusing on 50 "Hope Spots" — a global network of places around the world where it hopes to focus its ocean protection efforts. The end goal is to protect 20% of the world's oceans by 2020.

There are a number of additional organizations, foundations and trusts working to save the ocean, including:

  • The Nature Conservancy

  • Oceana

  • The Marine Conservation Institute

  • The Ocean Conservancy

  • The World Wide Fund for Nature

  • Reef Check Worldwide

  • The International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN)

  • The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)

  • The Fisheries and Aquaculture Department of the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO)

How are marine protected areas actually enforced?

Monitoring large swathes of the sea to ensure their protection is challenging, so how can we actually do it?

That's the $20 billion question — and the answer, suggested by the UK's recent move, may lie in innovative technology.

The UK, for example, is using much of its proposed budget for the Pitcairn Islands Marine Reserve to set up a technologically advanced watch room, where full-time staff members would use new private sector satellite technologies to track and follow mariners who violate the reserve's rules. The initiative, dubbed Project Eyes on the Seas, is a partnership between satellite company Satellite Applications Catapult and Pew's Global Ocean Legacy, with support from the Swiss-based Bertarelli Foundation.

In the past — and this is still the case in many other places around the world — marine protected areas have been monitored through expensive boat patrols to stop overfishing. Satellite technology can be more cost-effective and efficient.

The future of these efforts, at least currently, seems to lie in strategic partnerships between conservation organizations, NGOs, private satellite companies, authorities and governments.

What can you do?

Now that you're armed with basic information about marine protected areas and their importance, you can take steps to help ensure their successful implementation.

Here's how to start: Make sustainable seafood choices, reduce your carbon footprint, and use less plastic.

You can also support the organizations mentioned above, find out what marine protected areas exist in your area and contact your elected officials about increasing their scope, and participate in local beach cleanups.

If everyone did his or her part, we could finally get closer to saving and restoring what Sylvia Earle calls the "blue heart of the planet."
