Former Nazi prisoner of war camp which housed SS fanatics to be transformed into modern holiday hotspot for tourists 

  • A £600,000 project has been proposed to transform Cultybraggan Camp in Scotland into tourist accommodation
  • A number of notorious Nazis were locked up and killed at the settlement during the 1940s 
  • Also known as Camp No 21 it is the last remaining example of a purpose-built PoW camp in the UK 

ByBecky Pemberton For Mailonlineand Victoria Woollaston

Published: 11:53 GMT, 11 June 2015 | Updated: 12:30 GMT, 11 June 2015

From tours to the Chernobyl disaster site, to seeing where Jack the Ripper stalked the streets in London, 'dark tourism' has taken off - with the latest addition to the trend being a former high-security Nazi prisoner of war camp in Scotland.

Plans for a £600,000 project to transform a camp infamous for holding fanatical Nazis into modern tourist accommodation are under way. 

Cultybraggan Camp in Perthshire has applied to feature seven refurbished B-listed Nissen huts - where a number of notorious Nazis were locked up during the 1940s.

The Cultybraggan Camp, near Comrie in Perthshire, Scotland, was constructed in 1939 and could soon be transformed into modern tourist accommodation

It housed up to 40,000 German and Italian prisoners, including, according to legend, Adolf Hitler's deputy, Rudolf Hess

The heritage centre has been opened to give people a sense of its wartime past as well as the history of the surrounding area

The ominous camp held those considered most committed to the Nazi cause, including, it's said, Hitler's deputy Rudolf Hess (pictured)

The ominous camp held those considered most committed to the Nazi cause, including Hitler's deputy Rudolf Hess, who is said to have briefly stayed there after he parachuted into the country, though some dispute this story. 

Also known as Camp No 21, it is the last remaining example of a purpose-built PoW camp in the UK and the best-preserved prisoner of war (PoW) camp in Scotland.

In the most notorious incident in the camp's history, five Nazi prisoners held there murdered a fellow prisoner, Sergeant Wolfgang Rostberg, whose zeal for the Nazi cause had, they believed, waned in captivity.

The five were tried, found guilty, and eventually hanged at Pentonville Prison.

Owners of the 64-acre camp, Comrie Development Trust (CDT), have said they are offering community shares to local residents to support the venture.

They hope the transformation plans are seen as a way to retain the history of the area while also boosting the local economy.

Guests will be able to stay in self-catered and bunkhouse accommodation in the huts, meaning they can sleep where notorious PoWs spent their time.

CDT chairwoman Emma Margrett said: 'This is a really promising project and has only been possible due to the foresight of the community when they bought the camp.

'The buildings will be restored to a condition as stipulated by historic Scotland, which has given its backing to the project, offering a grant of up to £257,500.

'Historic Scotland's grant offer is conditional upon proposals being approved and match funding being obtained.'

The village has been a popular haven for dramatic re-enactments of the camp's prisoner of war era, with marches through Comrie to Cultybraggan.

Historic Scotland has offered the project a conditional grant of £257,500, with the total cost for the building work estimated at £578,000.

A spokesman for Historic Scotland, said: 'We have received an application through our Building Repair Grant scheme from the Comrie Development Trust regarding proposed development plans at the Cultybraggan site, in Perthshire.

'The application for grant assistance relates to the re-development of seven, B-listed WWII structures into self-catering accommodation.

HOW FEAR FOR A DYSTOPIAN FUTURE FUELS DARK TOURISM AROUND THE WORLD 

Research led by Associate Professor Jeffrey Podoshen from Franklin and Marshall College in Pennsylvania sought to explore why humans are so fascinated with learning about violence, cruelty and death and seeing its effects first hand.

Their findings suggested it may be our way of preparing for a dystopian future - or even death - by coming face-to-face with our biggest fears. 

This includes uncertainty around global politics, violence from groups such as ISIS, developments and policies about nuclear war and the rise of artificial intelligence and machines.

The researchers believe that when this is combined with sadistic tendencies - which previous studies have suggested are held by many members of society - and our insecurities about death it forms a morbid fascination.

Visiting these sites creates so-called 'stimulation and emotional contagion' that lets people experience death and violence with a group.

When in a group, this makes them feel safer while coming face-to-face with something that makes them feel unsafe.

And all of these create the desire to visit dystopian dark tourist sites.

Plus, the researchers said that familiarising themselves and seeing death 'first hand' helps people come to terms with the idea of dying.

The ringleaders of the infamous 1944 Devizes Plot to free 250,000 PoWs from camps throughout the country - and then mount an attack on the UK from within - were consigned to Camp 21, as Cultybraggan was known during the war years

'This is a unique project, which will see a number of these corrugated metal structures brought back in to use and enhance the existing refurbishment work that has been carried out to date by the local trust as part of wider plans for mixed use development at the site.

'Whilst we have accepted the application in principle, we will now look to fully assess the requirements of this project before confirming a final amount of grant funding.'

Named PoW camp No 21, Cultybraggan was built in 1941 to house 4,000 prisoners at a time, many of them the toughest Afrika Korps and SS troops.

In 1949 it became a training camp for the Army, and in 1960 was turned into a Cold War monitoring post, with a bunker completed in 1990.

The site, the last remaining example of a purpose-built PoW camp in the UK, has been owned by the locally-run Comrie Development Trust since it exercised a community right-to-buy option in 2007

There was a fear that the site would be used for housing, out of keeping with the community, when it was put up for sale by the Government for £350,000. Instead it was purchased by the Trust which has strived to give the camp a new lease of life

The complex was retained as a training camp after the war and regulars, territorials and Army cadets were frequent visitors for the next 60 years until the Ministry of Defence closed it down in 2004

The 64-acre camp still contains numerous Nissan huts and buildings and even a high-tech underground bunker, making it the most complete prisoner-of-war camp remaining in the UK

As part of the funding individuals are being invited to invest in community shares which, it is hoped, will raise £35,000
