About the Met Office Weather Warnings

BBC Weather carries two types of weather warnings issued by the Met Office: Warnings and Early Warnings.

Warnings will be issued when severe weather is expected within the next 24 hours.

Early Warnings will be issued more than 24 hours ahead of severe weather.

There are three categories of event Red, Amber and Yellow - the most severe is Red.

A Warning and an Early Warning of the same colour have the same severity but are forecast to arrive at different times. Thus, the difference between a Red Warning and a Red Early Warning is the lead time of the event.

When a warning is in force, full information can be found at Met Office Weather Warnings

About Flood Warnings

The flood warnings are issued by the Environment Agency, the Scottish Environment Protection Agency and Natural Resources Wales, and sent to the BBC Weather Centre. We then issue a compendium of warnings based on the latest information available. When severe flood warnings are issued they will also be highlighted on TV broadcasts.

Find out more about Flood Warnings

There are a number of ways you find out whether your area is at risk from flooding. The Environment Agency, the Scottish Environment Protection Agency and Natural Resources Wales update their warnings 24 hours a day via the Floodline number.

Monthly Outlook

Monday 19 October Published at 10:00

Monthly Outlook

Summary
High and mostly dry so far this October

High pressure has dominated our weather over the UK for much of this month. Rainfall accumulations are considerably down on average for the first half of October. The quiet, settled weather is in marked contrast to our more traditional Octobers when we often see deep areas of low pressure rushing across the Atlantic bringing spells of wet and very windy weather. In fact over the past week, we have seen the anniversary of two major storms which affected the British Isles. Last Wednesday was the 10th anniversary of a severe storm across western Scotland which brought gusts of over 100mph resulting in loss of life on South Uist in the Outer Hebrides. Friday marked the 28th anniversary of the great storm which caused widespread destruction and loss of life across the south of England. Just after 3am on 16th October 1987 a wind gust of 115mph was recorded at Shoreham in West Sussex.

Monday 19 October—Sunday 25 October
The week starting on a high, but ...

It does look as though we will switch back to the other side of autumn as we go through this week. Still a mostly settled start with Monday and Tuesday staying mainly dry with some sunshine. Gradually as the high pressure slips southwards, it will allow weather fronts to bring rain into the north along with strengthening westerly winds.

The first pulse of heavier rain looks set to affect western Scotland later on Tuesday and into Wednesday but the rain becoming light and patchy as it makes its way southwards. After a period of mostly light winds, it will become very windy across the north during Wednesday and into Thursday with severe gales possible across the north of Scotland.

A drier, brighter cooler interlude will follow on Thursday before further outbreaks of rain reach Scotland and Northern Ireland later on Friday, edging southwards over the weekend.

The change in the wind direction will be most noticeable across the south of England. During the past week, we have been feeling the effects of cold air feeding all the way from Russia across Central Europe and has been quite chilly when you are outside for any length of time. The Atlantic westerly wind will have a milder feel with less layers required as you step outside.

Monday 26 October—Sunday 1 November
Will the weather be tricking or treating?

That very much depends on where you live. It looks like a changeable weather pattern for more north and western parts of the British Isles with deep areas of low pressure crossing the Atlantic and, at this stage, they are expected to pass to the northwest of Scotland during the week. They will bring spells of wet and windy weather, especially to more northern parts of the UK.

High pressure will never be too far away from the southeast during the week so still some dry weather with a little sunshine. Weather fronts and associated bands of rain will make their way southwards across the country during the week but the heaviest rain will always be in the northwest. Temperatures will be fairly close to normal for the end of October and it will be windy at times with severe gales possible in the northwest.

Monday 2 November—Sunday 15 November
Change in month but not much change in weather.

As we go into the first two weeks of November, we will continue to see weather systems pushing in from the Atlantic. They will bring spells of wet and windy weather interspersed by drier, brighter and colder interludes. The weather fronts will be most active across more northern and western parts of the British Isles with the heaviest rain and the strongest winds here. The driest and brightest weather will continue to be across the south and east with these quieter conditions extending up across the rest of the country from time to time.

Next week

Will the high make a return?

Monthly forecasting
The weather beyond about a week ahead stretches even the most experienced weather forecaster. Complex numerical weather forecast models from the Met Office and the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) are run many times for the month (and season) ahead to build up a picture of the likelihood of different weather types affecting the UK.
