Philae, Europe's comet lander, wakes up after seven months

Jubilation at European Space Agency as signal picked up after comet lander goes into sleep when solar-powered battery ran flat in November

By Sarah Knapton, Science Editor, video source ITN

6:00PM BST 14 Jun 2015

Follow

Comments

A comet probe which ran out of power seven months ago, leading scientists to fear that crucial data about the early solar system had been lost, has woken up and is now communicating with Earth.

There was jubilation at the European Space Agency on Saturday night when a signal from Philae was picked up at 10.28pm.

For 85 seconds, Philae "spoke" with its team on ground, via spaceship Rosetta which dropped off the lander in November and has been orbiting comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko ever since.

Already hundreds of packets of data which were feared lost forever have been sent back to Earth for analysis.

• What do you do when your landing probe bounces into a crater?

“It’s good to hear from the lander. That’s somewhat of an understatement,” said Rosetta project scientist Dr Matt Taylor,

“I always knew it would come back. It’s like a soap opera. We had our cliff hanger, and now it’s got going again. I am very happy.

“It’s all to play for now. We have to try and ascertain its condition in the short term. We have got some good inferences of its health .It’s just woken up so it is not doing any science yet. But if everything goes according to plan we will start looking at doing the operations we were trying to do in November.”

Scientists think the Philae lander landed in the shadow of a cliff (top right). The cross marks the location of the intended landing spot (ESA)

The probe was sent to 67P to look for vital clues about the origin of life on Earth because comets bombarding our planet are thought to have brought with them large amounts of water and complex organic compounds.

But the 25-year mission was thrown into jeopardy last year after Philae bounced away from its landing site and became stuck under the shadow of a cliff where it’s solar panels could not pick up enough light to power the little lander.

On November 15, the European Space Agency said that Philae had "fallen into idle mode and admitted it could be ‘a possible long silence".

Before its power ran out after about 60 hours, the lander managed to obtain some samples for analysis, but crucially failed to deploy its ice pick, drill and hammer which would have revealed the comet’s chemical make-up.

Although some of the information was sent back to Earth, much of it was stuck in the probe’s memory.

However, the team had hoped that as the comet moved close to the Sun, and the southern hemisphere entered its "Summer" season, there would be enough power for the lander to fire back up and begin communicating.

The ESA announced the news via Philae’s own Twitter account:

Hello Earth! Can you hear me? #WakeUpPhilae

— Philae Lander (@Philae2014) June 14, 2015

Then in a tweet directed at the account for its mothership, the space agency said:

Hello @ESA_Rosetta! I'm awake! How long have I been asleep? #Lifeonacomet

— Philae Lander (@Philae2014) June 14, 2015

It replied saying it had been a seven-month rest, before the Philae account expressed surprise:

Wow @ESA_Rosetta! That’s a long time? time for me to get back to work! #Lifeonacomet

— Philae Lander (@Philae2014) June 14, 2015

Oh, OK @ESA_Rosetta! I’m still a bit tired anyway? talk to you later! Back to #lifeonacomet!

— Philae Lander (@Philae2014) June 14, 2015

Intriguingly it appears that the little probe may have been awake, and operating for some time, before making contact with Rosetta, giving scientist months of information to study.

ESA scientist Mark McCaughrean said: "It's been a long seven months, and to be quite honest we weren't sure it would happen - there are a lot of very happy people around Europe at the moment."

"I think we're optimistic now that it's awake that we'll have several months of scientific data to pore over.”

Scientists are now waiting for the next contact which could be several days away. Once established the team are likely to begin drilling into the comet to learn more about its physical make-up.

The Rosetta probe took 10 years to reach the comet and managed to send back some images of its surroundings before it lost power. Scientists are still unsure exactly where the lander but radio contact should now allow precise coordinates to be determined.

Comet 67P is currently 205 million km (127 million miles) from the Sun, and will reach its nearest point in August before heading back in to the far reaches of the solar system.

However, as it approaches the Sun, it becomes more active - throwing out huge clouds of gas and dust which could interfere with Philaes instruments or prevent communication with Rosetta.

The mission was due to end in September, but the European Space Agency said it was likely it would now run to 2016. When Rosetta runs out of fuel, the team is hoping to land Rosetta next to Philae on the comet.

Professor Monica Grady, who was part of the Open University team that developed one of Philae’s instruments, posted a message on Twitter:

Fingers trembling too much to type quickly. I only have the news feeds that everyone else has. But so happy! @ESA_Rosetta

— Monica Grady (@MonicaGrady) June 14, 2015

Shortly after its historic landing, Philae managed to conduct experiments and send data to earth for about 60 hours before its batteries were depleted and it was forced into hibernation.

In January, Esa called off the dedicated search for the probe.


A picture taken by the Philae lander of 67P/CG as it descends onto the surface of the comet (Reuters)

Scientists still do not know precisely where Philae touched down on 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko on November 12, after bouncing twice on the icy comet when its anchoring system failed.

Studies of images taken by the orbiter have shown Philae crossed a large depression named "Hatmehit" before coming to rest at an angle close to the wall of a crater or cliff.

Philae's landing on a fast-moving comet 300 million miles away has been hailed as one of humankind's greatest scientific achievements.
