The day Mum said,
‘I’m gay’

illustrations by Daniel Doran

It is probably the hardest conversation of their life. But for many men and women, telling their family they’re gay is the alternative to living a lifelong lie. Here, families who have faced the fallout of coming out share their stories

by Sally Williams

Names and some identifying details have been changed

Lydia Doyle, 21, is a student. Her father, a businessman, left her mother, an interior designer, 14 years ago, after 20 years together. He now lives with his partner, Alan, who works for a computer company. Lydia’s brother, Sam, 24, is also a student

I was seven; my brother 10. We were playing inside after school and I saw Mum and Dad sitting on swings in the back garden. I went out to play with them, but got yelled at to go back inside again. About five minutes later they came in and said they wanted to speak to us. Dad basically said, “I’m gay.” I remember being quite bossy and saying, “Of course I know what being gay means.” I had absolutely no idea.

My parents’ relationship had always seemed good. They met at school when they were 17 and had been together more or less ever since. Mum only found out the week before us. Dad just came up to her one day and announced it. He’d been away on a business trip. Everyone got drunk at a dinner and he just blurted it out – to a person who knew Mum. He panicked that she would find out, so decided to tell her.

Dad moved to a flat 20 minutes away and we saw him every other weekend. We live in a small town and soon everyone knew why he had left. When Brokeback Mountain came out, someone said to Mum, “We should go and see that. It’s like your life story.” That really upset her.

I was quite laid-back, too young to really understand. Sam, on the other hand, didn’t speak to our father for five or six years. Sometimes he’d lie on the floor holding Mum’s ankles, crying, refusing to see Dad. He was angry with him for hurting Mum and lying to us. Sam thinks Dad must have known he was gay, so why marry her? I’ve never really talked to Dad about it but I think he thought he was gay, but wasn’t sure and was in denial.

Three years after he told us, Dad moved to London, where he now lives with his long-term boyfriend. Mum started her own company and is quite successful. Her focus became Sam and me. She’s not happy. She hasn’t found anyone else. She’s said to me many times that if Dad had told her he was gay earlier, she would happily have been friends with him. It’s the fact that they got married and had children.

Sam still struggles with it. When he was younger he used to be really easy to talk to. After Dad left he kept everything to himself and he’s still like that, but I don’t know if that is because of what happened.

I speak to Dad on the phone about twice a week and text him every day. I love him so much. But I’ve learned not to trust people. You can’t ever really know anyone, even those you think you know well.

Eve Clarke, 29, is an administrator. Her father, an architect, left her mother, a nurse, 17 years ago after 17 years of marriage. He now lives with his partner, Mark, a drama teacher. Eve’s brother, Charlie, 31, is an accountant

I’d just had my 12th birthday when Mum and Dad sat us down after
Sunday dinner and said, “We’ve made a decision. We’re not going to stay together as a couple. But your dad is still going to live with us for a bit.”
They were childhood sweethearts and got married in their early 20s.
But for the last few years they’d been sleeping in separate rooms. There was never any friction or arguments. Even after they said they were splitting up it was very calm. Mum went out a lot more, that was all.

A year later Dad moved out and we would stay with him at weekends. Then, when I was 17, he told us he was having a relationship with a man. And we said, “We know!” He never talked about women, only ever his “friend”. Mark was always there at weekends. He’d pop in and stay at least one night. Dad would say Mark had crashed for the night rather than driving home. My brother would say, “I’m sure Dad’s gay.” And I’d think, “No, he can’t be. Dad is not flamboyant or camp at all.” But as I got older, I became more aware of relationships and sex and it was obvious.

I’m very close to my brother and so after Dad told us we fed off each other’s reactions. We were both like, “Well, he’s not changed. He is still who we’ve known him to be.”

Dad says it just grew inside him and it was something he tried to fight. His dad was very much a man’s man: you don’t cry, you do what you’re told – that sort of thing. He worked on the railway and was a big influence on Dad. He died when I was about seven. But it took Dad a few years to take that big step and I think Mum really helped him. She says she sort of always knew that he didn’t do it to hurt her. And that maybe she should have let him go sooner. She would always say, “Don’t judge people by their colour or how they dress; everyone is equal.”

I sometimes wonder if she did that knowing what might lie ahead.
Dad met Mark quite a few years after he split up from my mum. They
met online – he’s not an extrovert and doesn’t do the clubbing thing, so I don’t think he knew where to find someone. Mum met another man and they married last year and they all get on. In fact, we all go on holiday together. It’s lovely, really.

Shelley Clarke, 43, is a teacher. Her mother, a housewife, left her father, a civil servant, 26 years ago after 23 years of marriage. She now lives with her partner, Mary, a policewoman. Shelley has two older brothers and is married to Dan, also a teacher, with whom she has two daughters, aged seven and 10

I don’t recall many arguments between my parents but they weren’t a particularly loving couple. They had separate interests – Dad, golf, and mum, her friends. So when they told me they were splitting up, it wasn’t really a surprise. I was 17 and they sat me down and were very matter-of-fact. I’d stay with Mum, Dad would move out. My brothers had left home.

The following year, Mum and I were watching EastEnders and she suddenly said, “By the way, I’ve found somebody.” And then she said it was a woman. I didn’t hear anything else after that. My world was all about boys. They were all my friends talked about. At school we’d say, “That’s girl’s a lezzer because she’s got no friends.” I thought, “I can’t tell anybody. No one will speak to me again.” This was the late 1980s, the time of the Don’t Die of Ignorance adverts. So to me, Mum saying, “I’m gay,” meant dying.

Suddenly I felt she wasn’t my mum and I couldn’t trust her. I went off the rails. I stayed out all night, anything not to go home. I guess I wanted to punish her.

Things changed when a teacher noticed something was awry and asked if I was OK. Eventually I told him and he was amazing. He let me be angry, then said, “This is OK. There are other lesbians in the world. Your mum has found someone she loves and is happy.”

I got into university but by this time I was estranged from Mum. I knew she would have been so proud to see me there, but I was like, no, you’re not having any part of this. At uni, I met someone who is gay and he introduced me to all kinds of people. Every time I told someone and they accepted it, it helped me come to terms with it a bit more.

It wasn’t until I was 33, married and pregnant, that the turning point came. That’s when you really need your mum, and I didn’t want my kids to grow up not knowing her. I called her and asked her to come around. I said, “Bring Mary with you.” She did and we talked and cried and I apologised.

Now the girls are closer to Mum and Mary than to my dad and his new wife. I worried about what to tell the girls, but my husband was amazing. He said, “You got lucky. You’ve got Nana and her special nana.”

I don’t think you can predict how you will behave until it happens to you. I would describe myself as a very non-judgemental person, except, it seems, when it came to my own mum – when I became the most judgemental person in the world. I often wonder if I would have handled it differently if it was my dad who was gay. Obviously the stigma is still there, but I think I might have been a bit more OK with it.

Sarah Mason, 75, is a retired librarian and writer. She left her husband, a civil servant, and their two children, over 40 years ago. She lives with her partner of 34 years, Kitty, 73, a retired solicitor

I was 19 when I got married. He seemed nice and kind, but I found him difficult to talk to. I’d had crushes on older girls at school and understood from women’s magazines that this was a phase. But I never grew out of it and that was very disturbing, so I felt it was imperative that I got married.

My son arrived after a year; my daughter four years after that. I enjoyed being a mother. I tried hard. I made sure they had books and things to do like painting, sewing and cutting out.

In 1973 I met a young woman and we had a tremendous amount in common. We didn’t have a relationship – I was good with a capital G – but I learned a lot from her. She was a feminist and I read things she recommended. She used the word lesbian in conversation and it was like breaking a taboo. I started looking at my marriage, my kids, my life. I started wanting to feel less lonely.

For about three months I hardly slept. And then on 17 July, at the age of 34, after another day of struggling, I suddenly said to myself, I’m a lesbian. That date is written in my diary every year.

I joined support groups and met up with other married lesbian women.
It meant trips to London, which was difficult as I had no money of my own. I had to account for money to my husband so I lied to him and said I’d been to meetings connected with church.

Eventually it got to a point where I thought, “I’ve got to go.” The children were in their early teens. It was very, very painful. I told my husband and he didn’t take me seriously. I got myself a room in London. He said he’d tell the children and, the damn fool that I was, I let him. I don’t know exactly what he told them but I’ve got an awful feeling it was something that made a lot of difference – like, your mother is fed up with us.

From then on it was difficult to see them. I’d have to go back to the marital home, and he insisted on being present. Then my daughter refused to see me and I never knew why. I think she’d been told that if she didn’t see me I’d come back. I last saw them more than 30 years ago – not long after I’d met Kitty. Without her I wouldn’t have got this far. We’re not dependent on each other. We enlighten each other.

I live in hope but I have no expectations that I will ever see my children again. I’ve had a lot of depression and therapy. I recently had a really good counsellor. She made me realise I had to accept it. I have got compensations. Maybe they have too.
