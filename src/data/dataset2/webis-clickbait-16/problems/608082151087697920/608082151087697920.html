Apple Music's rivals aren't impressed

1.4k
Shares
Share
Tweet
Share
What's This?
By Adario Strange2015-06-09 01:11:22 UTC

Apple isn't accustomed to being on the outside looking in when it comes to being one of the cool kids. But on Monday, when the company unveiled Apple Music, it seemed like all the popular music apps decided to pile on Apple's attempt to reinvent its music offering.

The first blow came from the CEO and founder of one of the most successful music services on the planet, Spotify. Following the announcement of Apple Music's features, Daniel Ek tweeted a simple, sarcastic response: "Oh ok." His missive was quickly deleted, but not before screenshots had been taken.

See also: Apple Music vs. the streaming competition

The next, and arguably the most biting response, came from Rdio. Copying Apple's old advertisement from 1981, mocking IBM's attempt to enter the personal computer space, Rdio tweeted, "Welcome, Apple. Seriously." As in the original ad, the title was followed by a message, which in this case called Apple Music a latecomer to the party.

Welcome, Apple. Seriously. #applemusic pic.twitter.com/AnoeKZ5ZIa

— Rdio (@Rdio) June 8, 2015

Next in the schadenfreude parade was Slacker Radio — offering a less contentious, but nevertheless critical tweet that called Apple Music out for being late to the race.

Great radio = artists + DJs + curation. Welcome to the party, @AppleMusic: http://t.co/LihSRb09kV #WWDC15 @Beats1 pic.twitter.com/4qmvfYuhKl

— Slacker Radio (@SlackerRadio) June 8, 2015

Finally, a service called 8Tracks kicked a bit of dirt on Apple Music by responding to a tweet from a person critical of Apple's new service. "[W]e don’t need one celebrity DJ when we have millions of people like you making beautiful playlists," read the message on the company's official feed, which has 68,000 followers.

HA, true…but we don’t need one celebrity DJ when we have millions of people like you making beautiful playlists. https://t.co/IfyigzmEfq

— 8tracks (@8tracks) June 8, 2015

Of course, none of this means that Apple Music is doomed, or that it won't displace some of its rivals. But what it does tell us is that Apple's early success with iTunes won't automatically translate into a win in the new landscape of streaming music services.

Normally, other than behemoths like Samsung, most companies avoid taking shots at Apple. Now that honeymoon is over — and Apple Music is in store for the same kind of sharp elbows delivered to Tidal in recent weeks.

Let the new music wars begin.
