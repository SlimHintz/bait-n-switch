'I’m looking forward to getting home': Eleanor Hawkins flies back to Britain

British tourist who caused uproar in Malaysia by stripping on top of a mountain speaks of relief, as local community leaders say backpackers should have faced native court

Eleanor Hawkins pcitured at Kota Kinabalu's airport Photo: Julian Simmonds

By Harriet Alexander, Kuala Lumpur

5:09PM BST 13 Jun 2015

Follow

A British backpacker who caused uproar in Malaysia by stripping on top of a mountain has spoken of her relief as she flew back to the UK after being deported.

Eleanor Hawkins, a 23-year-old aeronautical engineering graduate, was due to arrive at Heathrow in the early hours of Sunday morning, having flown in from the Borneo coast via Kuala Lumpur.

Eleanor Hawkin had spent three days in jail (Julian Simmonds)

“I’m looking forward to getting home,” she said on Saturday night, awaiting her connecting flight in the Malaysian capital.

When asked how she felt about supposedly angering the Mountain Gods with her topless antics, she smiled and shook her head, refusing to reply.

And yet for those left behind in Borneo, there was anger that the question had not been answered, with community leaders saying that she should be immediately extradited back to Malaysia to face a native court.

Some said that a June 5 earthquake on the mountain, which killed 18 people, was because the spirits that guard the sacred hilltop were offended by their prank.

“I’m so angry they have been allowed to leave,” said Nor Azlan, the mountain guide who led with the tourists. “They should be sent back to Malaysia.”

Daikin Anam, the 32-year-old park ranger who made the initial police complaint, agreed, saying he was “disappointed” that they had been freed. “It’s not right,” he said. “They got off too lightly.”

Miss Hawkins, a former head girl of Ockbrook School in Derbyshire, pleaded guilty to indecency on Friday before a court in Kota Kinabalu, the coastal city in northern Borneo, capital of Sabah state.

Briton Eleanor Hawkins (inset) was one of five tourists arrested in Malaysia following the naked photo stunt

She admitted that on May 30 she and nine others had dared each other to take their clothes off near the summit of Mount Kinabalu – a sacred mountain for the Dasun people - and withstand the chill on top of the 13,435ft peak.

But when photos of the prank emerged on social media, a formal complaint was made by Mr Daikin. Miss Hawkins and the other three were arrested on Tuesday, remanded in custody on Wednesday, and sentenced on Friday evening. Several hours later, in the midst of a tropical lightning storm, another large earthquake – registering 6.0 on the Richter scale – struck the mountain region. This time, however, no one was injured.

The straight-A student was escorted out of Kota Kinabalu airport on Saturday morning with three other Westerners – Canadian siblings Lindsey and Danielle Petersen, aged 22 and 23, and 23-year-old Dutchman Dylan Snel.

Covering her face, she was ushered through by embassy representatives. And despite her ordeal, she did manage a slight grin at the attention her presence aroused.

A poster advertising Dunkin Donuts inside the airport may have raised another smile: “DD was here (Mount Kinabalu)” it read, with an image of a group of climbers tucking into the sugary snacks on the sacred mountain.

“She was very relieved when she knew she’d be leaving,” said Ronny Cham, their lawyer. “They weren’t scared. They had been treated alright.”

Miss Hawkins flew in economy class on both legs of her flight.

The tourists were sentenced to pay a 5,000 ringgit (£858) fine and spend three days in jail, which – taking into account the time already in detention - expired at midnight on Friday. They spent Saturday morning with the immigration authorities, before being taken to the airport.

Miss Hawkins’ father Tim told The Telegraph that his daughter, who left the UK in January to travel alone throughout South East Asia, had intended to travel onwards from Malaysia to Singapore and Hong Kong. But instead she was flying directly home to Draycott, in Derbyshire.

He said the sentence was "appropriate and fair for the offence committed," and that he was looking forward to welcoming her home.

“I’m not surprised at how quickly they processed the case,” said Mr Cham. “The important thing is that the embassy and the parents took my advice – to plead guilty and offer an apology.

“This was their best bet.”

Mr Cham said that foreigners in Malaysia would not be granted bail, because they needed two local people to provide surety. If they contested the charges, they would have faced “months, even years” awaiting trial, he added.

And he said the group were fortunate to escape prosecution from the native court.

Tribal people in Sabah had threatened to haul the four before their own judge, where they would have faced unspecified jail time or fines. Ten head of buffalo was suggested by one elder as a suitable punishment – with one buffalo costing around 1,500 ringgits (£260).

“The native court could have issued a summons,” said Mr Cham. “The four were lucky to have been released on a Saturday, when the native court wasn’t sitting.”

Mr Nor, the mountain guide, said that he has been called before the native court on Tuesday. In his statement, given to police last week, Mr Nor described the incident as “disgraceful” and said he had never seen anything like it before.

“I told them not to be naked on Mount Kinabalu, because it’s not appropriate,” he said. “I told them other groups of tourists were nearby and they may feel offended – but they wouldn’t listen.”

He said that no one in the group asked whether it was acceptable.

“The girls were not so wilful. They were just half-naked, they didn’t take off their underwear.

“But the guys were stubborn. When they didn’t take my advice I could only leave them and walk away.”

On Saturday night he told The Telegraph that the four should have received the full three-month prison term.

“Three days in prison is far too light a term,” he said. “I will tell the native court what has happened – but they have just run away. It should not have been allowed.”

Mr Daikin, who initiated the legal proceedings, echoed the call for the four to be sent back to face the native court.

“They should have made a public apology,” he said. “They should have been brave, and faced the people of Sabah.

"They should have been as brave in court as they were on top of the mountain.”
