          Cardale Jones living the dream this summer

          6/10/2015

          CLEVELAND -- A smile as big as his right arm has seemingly been permanently affixed to his face for six months, and there’s no chance of it disappearing on this night when Cardale Jones arrives in a tunnel behind left field at Progressive Field.

          Picking just one event as a highlight out of everything that’s happened to one of the most unlikely success stories in recent college football history is impossible. And the Ohio State quarterback doesn’t even bother trying.

          Having President Barack Obama personally ask about his daughter? Delivering a message on the court about bringing another title to Ohio during a playoff game for his hometown Cavaliers? Showing up to a hastily called press conference and finding a packed gym waiting on his every word about his future?

          Jones wasn’t even supposed to be in this kind of position. He was a one-time third stringer. But then injuries put him on the field and wins in the Big Ten championship game, the Sugar Bowl and the inaugural College Football Playoff championship made him an overnight star.

          Now, making his way into an underground batting cage, wearing a black T-shirt that has his “12 Gauge” nicknamed embossed in gold and being greeted by every member of the Indians he passes, Jones is singularly focused on making the most of his latest opportunity in the spotlight.

          “I haven’t thrown a baseball in like two and a half years,” Jones admits while loosening up the arm that made him famous. “It’s coming back to me.”

          The ball comes out of his huge right hand effortlessly, sizzling through the air and making a big-league pop in the glove of a Cleveland staffer helping him prepare to throw out the ceremonial first pitch.

          Jones breaks a sweat and playfully debates throwing a curveball as part of what he jokes will be an audition for the team he grew up watching and the sport he loved first. As he makes his way up the dugout steps, a line instantly forms full of Ohio State fans wanting an autograph. Jones takes in a scene that can still seem like a dream given how quickly it has materialized -- and he keeps on smiling.

          A new normal for Jones

          There’s a routine now when Jones heads back to Cleveland, and his family and close friends are well aware of the drill.

          Once known around the nation almost exclusively for an ill-advised tweet about not coming to “play school,” Jones is now toasted for a national championship and draft stock that projects him as a first-round pick. And with a 6-foot-5, 250-pound frame, he’s an easy target for fans looking for a picture or an autograph.

          “It’s been kind of weird,” Jones said. “We try to go out when I get a chance to go back home to Cleveland on the weekends, and it’s weird when we’re walking through the mall and they’ve got to take every picture for me and stuff like that. They know the routine now, ‘Give me the camera, I’ll take it, I’ll take it.’ It’s kind of weird for them, too, because everybody is watching us when we’re sitting down to eat, people staring at us.

          “I understand it, and that’s the part of the fame that I could definitely live without, but I’m embracing it. I never, ever say no to anyone who wants to take a picture or if they want me to sign anything at any given time. I mean, it bugs me if I’m eating, but I’ll still do it. I understand what it means to them.”

          Even as recently as November, Jones could enjoy his privacy. J.T. Barrett, replacing two-time Big Ten player of the year Braxton Miller, who was injured in August, was soaking up the attention as a Heisman Trophy candidate leading the high-powered Ohio State offense. But Barrett’s fractured ankle in the regular-season finale against Michigan forced Jones into action, and the redshirt junior took the reins as seamlessly as he’s adjusted to being in front of cameras wherever he goes.

          “I see how happy this makes him,” Jones’ mentor and nonlegal guardian Michelle Nash said. “To see him doing what he loves and being recognized for it, it’s wonderful. Even when we went out to eat, it’s like nonstop. I think it’s a wonderful thing, but he was an amazing person before he threw that ball. People just don’t know that. They know him as the football player, I know him as the young man.

          “But this has all been a blessing.”

          Jones an open book on Twitter

          Never one to filter his thoughts, the added attention isn’t without some negatives that have nothing to do with finding a quiet spot to eat in public. For the highs and lows, the risks and rewards of using social media as a high-profile athlete, few know them all any better than Jones.

          This summer has been an example of that.

          He’s had to resort to a claim of being hacked for comments that offended female sports fans and forced to delete them after the fact. He's used his 140 characters to start a feud with NBA star Joakim Noah. He lobbied the Indians on Twitter to let him throw out the first pitch in Friday's game. It's also allowed him to share some moments of introspection reflecting on his achievements and a journey that hasn’t always been smooth with his more than 191,000 followers. Jones has given a glimpse of his softer side visiting children in the hospital (and of course, famously playing video games with some patients) and become a fixture of pictures with his handle tagged in them backing up a claim that the overnight celebrity will never turn down a photo request. He posts pics of himself and his daughter, Chloe, and his obsession with the other love of his life, Chipotle.

          But even after all that attention and scrutiny, praise or criticism, perhaps nothing hammered home the point to Jones that he can bring the world of college football to a standstill with just his cell phone than when he decided to overhaul his Twitter profile on May 1 with Akron logos and pretend he was transferring to play for the Zips.

          “That was the funniest [thing] in the world,” Jones said. “I probably shouldn’t have done that, and it just blew up my phone. I was like, ‘Holy s---, I just got an ESPN alert. Oh no, coach [Urban] Meyer is going to kill me.’

          “It blew up, everybody was calling and texting me, ‘What is going on?’ I tried not to answer the phone until I tweeted ‘May Fools.’ Seriously, I’m going to be honest, and I may be pretty stupid, but I swear I looked at my phone and it said April 1. I swear.”

          That wasn’t the first time Jones has backpedaled from one of his posts, and it probably won’t be the last. The redshirt junior has been an open book since his ill-advised post about “playing school” earned him a suspension from Meyer in 2012, a post that actually also earned him an inclusion in a textbook for freshmen at Ole Miss explaining the perils of social media. But his lack of filter, both on social media and in interviews, is a big part of the reason he’s built such a large audience.

          “I think people love it because they see that maybe a high-profile athlete doesn’t have to be all so serious all the time,” Jones said. “I think people appreciate me for who I am, appreciate my personality and can see how focused I am when it comes to game day.

          “But when it’s not game day, I’m still a human -- a kid at heart, and I love to have fun.”

          Few players are having more fun lately than Jones, and he’s not afraid to share it with the world. He certainly makes no effort to hide his personality or his ever-present grin when former Ohio State quarterback Troy Smith finds him outside the dugout at Progressive Field and the two Buckeyes start firing around a baseball.

          It definitely didn’t take long for new quarterbacks coach Tim Beck to find it out once he arrived to take over for Tom Herman this offseason, and the two appear to have quickly forged a strong relationship. That occasionally means Beck becomes the target of Jones’ personal brand of humor (read: firing a fastball in a cage just to scare to Beck).

          “He’s loose, he’s confident, he’s just a big kid," Beck said. "Really he is just a big kid who loves life and loves football. There are a lot of people in the world who probably wish they viewed life like he does. But as you saw in those three games, when it’s time to go to work, it’s time to go to work.

          “[Fame] can be a monster, there’s no question about it. But he’s been able to compartmentalize all of these things really, really well, and I think that’s why he’s been able to handle it so well so far.”

          Always something to smile about

          Regardless of what happens this fall, whether he is once again starting or back to being the third-string QB, Jones has etched his name into the record books forever already. He was among the unlikeliest of heroes on a storybook run to a national crown, and given how far he’s come since effectively being labeled a college football pariah in 2012, his celebrity status is still in some ways hard to fathom.

          Sitting on a couch outside the Ohio State locker room in yet another “12 Gauge” T-shirt, Jones himself seems to have a hard time processing it all. The moment doesn’t last long, though, and in a flash the corners of his mouth return to a familiar position.

          “Growing up you can’t even imagine being in that position,” Jones said. “Being center court at [Quicken Loans Arena] and everybody in the crowd knows who you are and they’re going crazy, and you’re selected to deliver a message. Or having a chance to meet President Obama and the first thing he asks you is, ‘How is Chloe?’ You know, things like that, you can’t imagine it and you can’t describe what you feel.

          “Everybody grows up wanting to win a national championship, win a Heisman, do this or do that, but you never really understand what it takes to do it. And then when you do it, how would you feel? That’s not like having your first girlfriend or your first kiss and you’re so happy. That comparison is not even close. When they come up with the word, I’ll definitely share it with you.”

          Until then, the look on his face that’s been there for the last six months will just have to suffice.
