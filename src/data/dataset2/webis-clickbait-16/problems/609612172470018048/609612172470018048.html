Snoop Dogg - the next CEO of Twitter?

Rapper puts himself forward to replace Dick Costolo as the boss of Twitter

Snoop Dogg Snoop, whose real name is Calvin Cordozar Broadus Jr, tweeted that he is 'ready to lead Twitter' Photo: Rex

By Andrew Trotman

7:22PM BST 12 Jun 2015

Follow

Comments

"If Snoop was Twitter CEO, the verified symbol wouldn't be a checkmark, it would be a weed leaf."

"If Snoop was Twitter CEO, it would be called 'Barker'."

Just two of the changes Twitter users suggested could happen if rapper Snoop Dogg became chief executive of the social network.

As unlikely as it sounds, the 43-year-old rapper has thrown his hat in the ring to replace outgoing boss Dick Costolo at the company.

Shortly after Mr Costolo announced he would be stepping down next month, Snoop, whose real name is Calvin Cordozar Broadus Jr, tweeted that he is "ready to lead Twitter". The musician, known for songs such as Drop it Like it's Hot and Gin & Juice, followed this with the hashtag #SnoopforCEO.

Im ready to lead @twitter !! #SnoopforCEO

— Snoop Dogg (@SnoopDogg) June 11, 2015

Snoop's push for the top job at Twitter was backed by some of his 12.3m followers, who started a new hashtag that swiftly became popular: #IfSnoopwasTwitterCEO.

His fans began tweeting what would happen if he was given the job:

#ifSnoopWasTwitterCEO the verified symbol wouldn't be a checkmark, it'd be a weed leaf. @SnoopDogg

— Trevor Schmitt (@T_Dogg_94) June 12, 2015

@SnoopDogg #ifSnoopWasTwitterCEO it'd be called Barker.

— Deviant Mugen (@Mugen_Metroid) June 12, 2015

Snoop, who has been nominated for 16 Grammy awards, followed up his original tweet with a fine display of business acumen. Announcing that his first task if hired would be to make the business more money, he was immediately given odds of 100/1 by Paddy Power to become the next boss of Twitter.

First order of Business! Get that Moolah! #SnoopforCEO @twitter

— Snoop Dogg (@SnoopDogg) June 11, 2015

Well @SnoopDogg we make you a 100/1 shot to be the Next Twitter CEO. Fancy a flutter?

— Paddy Power (@paddypower) June 12, 2015

Mr Costolo announced on Thursday night that he would step down on July 1, after coming under pressure following lacklustre results.

Investors initially supported the move, sending Twitter’s shares up 7.8pc in after-hours trading following the announcement. They fell 0.3pc to $35.75 on Friday.

Mr Costolo will be replaced in the interim by co-founder Jack Dorsey, chairman of Twitter and chief executive of Square, the mobile payments company he founded in 2009. He will also continue in both those roles.

However, Mr Dorsey will not be paid for leading Twitter. The company's SEC filing on Thursday stated that he had "agreed to forego any compensation for your role as interim chief executive officer".

He added on Thursday night that Mr Costolo's departure was his own decision and not related to the company's performance.

"I believe in the course the company is on," he told investors, adding that both internal and external candidates for the CEO position will be considered. "We’re looking for someone who uses and loves the product in every single way."

Mr Costolo believed the "scrutiny of the company" would "intensify" if he remained at the helm while a replacement was being found.

Anthony Noto, Twitter's chief financial officer, said Mr Costolo will not receive a severance package because he voluntarily stepped down.

"Unfortunately this news isn't surprising," said Nate Elliott of management consultant Forrester Research. "The bottom line is that Twitter isn't very good right now at serving either its users or its marketers."

Mr Dorsey was Twitter's chief executive and president from 2007 until 2008 but is now returning to lead a very different company.

Welcome back, @jack !! https://t.co/3papmyUKg0

— dick costolo (@dickc) June 11, 2015

Mr Costolo had been under pressure due to slowing user numbers, despite reporting 302m monthly active members in the first quarter, in line with expectations.

Revenues of $436m were up 74pc compared with 2014 but below its own forecast. Twitter, which has yet to make a profit since floating in November 2013, reported a loss of $162m, 23pc wider than a year earlier.

Full-year guidance was cut to between $2.17bn and $2.27bn, from the previous range of $2.3bn to $2.35bn.

Twitter lost a quarter of its value in April after the financial results were “leaked” while stock markets were still open.

Thank you for everything @dickc! You're a selfless leader who's built an amazing team and company #proud https://t.co/52z4TIYIUh

— Jack (@jack) June 11, 2015

The company's monthly user base will grow just 14.1pc this year, slowing from more than 30pc two years ago, according to research firm eMarketer. By 2019, its user growth rate will be just 6pc worldwide.

Despite the results revealing Twitter's weakest quarterly revenue growth as a publicly traded company, Mr Costolo defended the performance.

"It is still early days for these products, and we have a strong pipeline that we believe will drive increased value for direct response advertisers in the future," he said in a statement accompanying the earnings.

Twitter lists on the stock exchange in 2013. From left: Founders Jack Dorsey, Biz Stone, Evan Williams and Dick Costolo

Twitter monthly active user number is dwarfed by its main rival Facebook, which boasts 1.44bn.

On Tuesday, fellow Twitter co-founder Biz Stone laughed off suggestions the chief executive should resign, calling the idea "crazy".

"I don't actually know anything about those things and I tend to think in the longer term. You are talking about people who think quarterly and if [results are] a percent off that quarter, it's like: "Fire the CEO," and that's crazy," he told IBTimes UK.

Dick Costolo

"He weathers it well. Dick's got a thick skin and he used to be an improv comedian so it's like water off a duck's back with him. He just deals with it very well. He's very funny about it. He jokes about it, it doesn't seem to bother him."

Twitter floated two years ago at $26 per share, valuing the business at $14.2bn, in the most eagerly anticipated IPO since Facebook in 2012. The closed the day at $44.90, giving the company a market capitalisation of $31bn. It is now worth $23.5bn.

News of Mr Costolo's resignation came on the same day Twitter said it was removing its 140-character limit from direct messages between users. The limit is seen as a core part of the social network's appeal.
